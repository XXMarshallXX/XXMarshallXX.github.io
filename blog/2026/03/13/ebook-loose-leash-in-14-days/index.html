Chapter 1: Why Dogs Pull and What Loose-Leash Walking Really Means

If your dog pulls on the leash, you are not alone. Pulling is one of the most common challenges dog owners face, and it can turn a simple walk into a frustrating tug-of-war. The good news is that pulling does not mean your dog is stubborn, dominant, or trying to control you. In most cases, dogs pull because they have learned that pulling works. They want to get somewhere, sniff something, greet someone, or simply move faster than we do. Understanding why dogs pull is the first step toward changing the behavior.

Before you can teach loose-leash walking, it helps to know what your dog is experiencing on a walk. The outdoors is full of exciting smells, sounds, people, dogs, movement, and surprises. To your dog, a walk is not just exercise. It is information, stimulation, and opportunity. If your dog sees a tree, a squirrel, or another dog and lunges forward, that is often natural enthusiasm rather than disobedience. Training works best when you stop viewing pulling as “bad behavior” and start seeing it as a skill your dog has not learned yet.

Why Dogs Pull on the Leash

Dogs are naturally faster than humans, and they do not instinctively understand that leash pressure means “slow down” or “stay near me.” In fact, many dogs do the opposite. They feel pressure on the leash and lean into it. This is especially common in energetic dogs, young dogs, and breeds developed for pulling, chasing, or working at a distance from people.

One major reason dogs keep pulling is reinforcement. If your dog pulls toward a bush and gets to sniff it, pulling was rewarded. If your dog drags you toward another dog and gets to say hello, pulling was rewarded. If your dog pulls all the way down the sidewalk and eventually reaches the park, pulling was rewarded again. Dogs repeat behaviors that help them get what they want.

Other common reasons dogs pull include:

  • Excitement before and during walks
  • Lack of impulse control
  • Too much pent-up energy
  • Fear or stress that causes them to rush past something
  • Inexperience with leash walking
  • Inconsistent handling from person to person

It is also important to consider your dog’s age and history. Puppies often pull because everything is new and interesting. Adolescent dogs may pull because they are energetic and easily distracted. Rescue dogs may have never been taught leash skills at all. Even adult dogs who know how to walk nicely in one setting may pull in a more stimulating environment.

Your job is not to punish your dog for being a dog. Your job is to clearly teach the behavior you want and make that behavior worthwhile.

What Loose-Leash Walking Really Means

Loose-leash walking does not mean your dog must walk in perfect heel position every second of every walk. That expectation is unrealistic for most pet dogs and unnecessary for everyday life. A formal heel is a specific obedience behavior where the dog stays close to your side, often with intense focus. Loose-leash walking is more practical and more flexible.

Loose-leash walking means the leash stays relaxed most of the time. Your dog can move, sniff, and explore within the range you allow, but without constant tension. The goal is not robotic walking. The goal is connection, responsiveness, and calm movement together.

A dog who walks on a loose leash should be able to:

  • Stay aware of where you are
  • Slow down when needed
  • Follow your direction changes
  • Pause instead of dragging you forward
  • Check in with you regularly

This definition matters because many owners accidentally make the process harder by expecting too much too soon. If you insist that your dog stay glued to your left leg for an entire neighborhood walk, both of you may become frustrated. Instead, think of loose-leash walking as a partnership. Your dog gets some freedom, and you get a leash that is not tight.

That balance is what makes the skill sustainable. Dogs need opportunities to sniff and explore. Sniffing lowers stress, provides mental enrichment, and makes walks more enjoyable. A successful walk is not one where your dog never looks away from you. It is one where your dog can engage with the environment without pulling you through it.

Common Mistakes That Make Pulling Worse

Many owners unknowingly strengthen pulling because they are in a hurry or because they have not been shown a better method. The most common mistake is continuing to walk while the leash is tight. From your dog’s perspective, leash tension becomes part of the normal walking experience. If a tight leash still leads to forward progress, there is no reason for your dog to change.

Another mistake is being inconsistent. If you stop your dog from pulling one day but allow it the next, learning slows down. Dogs learn through repetition and clear patterns. If the rules change from walk to walk, your dog has to guess what works.

Watch out for these common problems:

  • Using walks only as physical exercise instead of also using them for training
  • Starting in busy, distracting environments before your dog is ready
  • Expecting too much duration too soon
  • Repeating cues like “easy” or “heel” without teaching what they mean
  • Holding the leash tightly the entire time, which creates constant tension
  • Skipping rewards once the dog shows small improvements

Equipment also matters, though it is not a substitute for training. A flat collar may be fine for some dogs, while others do better with a well-fitted front-clip harness or another humane management tool that gives the owner more control. The best equipment is the one that keeps your dog safe and helps you train without pain or fear. No tool teaches the skill by itself, but the right setup can make learning easier.

How to Set the Right Foundation Starting Today

The fastest way to improve leash walking is to stop rewarding pulling and start rewarding the position and pace you want. That does not require perfection. It requires timing, patience, and a clear plan.

Start with these practical steps:

  • Choose a low-distraction location such as your driveway, hallway, backyard, or a quiet sidewalk.
  • Bring small, high-value treats your dog can eat quickly.
  • Begin before your dog gets overexcited. Ask for calm at the door and wait for a moment of composure before heading out.
  • The moment the leash is loose, mark it with praise or a reward.
  • If the leash becomes tight, stop moving. Do not jerk back. Simply become still.
  • When your dog turns back, steps toward you, or creates slack in the leash, reward and move forward again.
  • Change direction often so your dog learns to pay attention to your movement.
  • Keep sessions short and successful rather than long and frustrating.

One especially helpful habit is rewarding check-ins. A check-in happens when your dog looks at you, comes back near you, or chooses to match your pace without being asked. These moments are easy to miss, but they are the building blocks of excellent leash manners. The more you reward them, the more often they happen.

It also helps to separate “training walks” from “decompression walks.” On a training walk, you focus on leash skills in a controlled setting. On a decompression walk, your dog may have more freedom on a longer leash in a safe area to sniff and explore. This prevents frustration and gives your dog a healthy outlet while you build better habits.

Most of all, be realistic. Loose-leash walking is not taught in one perfect session. It is built through many small repetitions. If your dog has spent months or years practicing pulling, you are not just teaching a new behavior. You are replacing an old one. Progress may come in stages: fewer lunges, shorter periods of tension, quicker check-ins, smoother turns. These are all signs that learning is happening.

Over the next chapters, you will learn exactly how to teach the skill step by step. For now, remember this: your dog is not giving you a hard time. Your dog is having a hard time in a world full of distractions, and with the right training, you can teach a calmer, more enjoyable way to walk together.

Key Takeaways

  • Dogs usually pull because it works, not because they are stubborn or dominant.
  • Loose-leash walking means a relaxed leash, not a strict competition-style heel.
  • Pulling is often reinforced when dogs reach what they want while the leash is tight.
  • Consistency matters: if the leash is tight, forward movement should pause.
  • Reward check-ins, calm pace, and any moment your dog chooses to keep the leash loose.
  • Start training in low-distraction areas and build up gradually.
  • Use humane equipment for safety and comfort, but remember that training creates the real change.

Chapter 2: The Right Gear, Rewards, and Setup for Training Success

Before you ask your dog to walk politely on a loose leash, you need the right foundation. Training success is not just about what you do during the walk. It is also about what your dog wears, what motivates them, and how carefully you set up the learning environment. Many loose-leash problems are made worse by poor equipment, weak rewards, or trying to train in places that are simply too distracting too soon.

In this chapter, you will learn how to choose safe and effective gear, how to use rewards that truly matter to your dog, and how to create a training setup that makes success much more likely. When these pieces are in place, your dog can focus, learn faster, and build good walking habits with far less frustration for both of you.

Choose Gear That Supports Learning

The goal of training equipment is not to force your dog into walking nicely. The goal is to give you safety, communication, and control while your dog learns a new skill. Good gear should be comfortable, secure, and easy to use. It should help prevent rehearsing bad habits without causing fear or pain.

For most dogs, a well-fitted front-clip harness or a standard flat collar paired with a regular leash is enough. A front-clip harness can be especially helpful for dogs that tend to pull hard, because it reduces leverage and helps redirect forward momentum without relying on force. A flat collar may work well for dogs who are already fairly calm and do not strain against the leash.

Whatever you choose, fit matters. A harness that rubs under the legs, shifts to one side, or allows the dog to back out of it will create problems. A collar that is too loose can slip off. A collar that is too tight is uncomfortable and distracting.

  • Use a regular leash, ideally 4 to 6 feet long.
  • Avoid retractable leashes for training. They teach constant tension and make clear communication harder.
  • Check buckles, clips, and stitching regularly for wear.
  • Make sure your dog can move naturally without chafing or pinching.
  • If your dog is strong or nervous, use extra care to ensure the gear is escape-proof.

Many owners ask whether special collars or corrective tools are necessary. For teaching loose-leash walking, the most effective long-term approach is to teach the dog what to do rather than relying on discomfort to stop pulling. Dogs learn best when they understand the desired behavior and are rewarded for it consistently. Equipment should support that process, not replace it.

Use Rewards Your Dog Actually Cares About

Loose-leash walking is difficult for many dogs because the outside world is full of competing rewards. Smells, movement, people, dogs, squirrels, and open space are often far more exciting than your voice alone. That is why rewards matter so much. If you want your dog to stay engaged with you, you need to bring something valuable to the lesson.

Food is often the easiest and most effective reward. Soft, small, high-value treats work best because they are quick to eat and easy to deliver often. Think tiny bits of chicken, cheese, turkey, or commercial training treats your dog loves. During early training, reward frequently. You are building a new habit, and habits grow through repetition.

Not every dog is equally food-motivated in every environment. Some dogs respond better to movement, praise, toys, or access to something they want. For example, if your dog loves sniffing, you can reward a few steps of loose-leash walking by saying, “Go sniff,” and letting them investigate a tree or patch of grass. If your dog loves greeting people, calm walking can become the path to that reward.

To find your dog’s best rewards, test them. Do not assume. Bring two or three treat options and notice which one your dog chooses first. Pay attention to how quickly they respond in different locations. A treat that works in your living room may be too boring on a busy sidewalk.

  • Use tiny treats so you can reward often without overfeeding.
  • Keep treats easy to reach in a pouch or pocket.
  • Match reward value to difficulty. Harder environments need better rewards.
  • Reward immediately when the leash is loose or your dog checks in with you.
  • Mix food rewards with life rewards like sniffing, moving forward, or praise.

Timing is just as important as reward quality. If the leash loosens and you wait too long, your dog may not understand what earned the reward. Mark the correct moment with a word like “Yes” or a clicker, then deliver the reward. This makes your communication clearer and speeds up learning.

Set Up the Environment for Success

One of the biggest mistakes owners make is starting loose-leash training in the hardest possible place: the front yard, the street, or a busy park. Your dog may be capable of learning, but not in a setting where every sound, smell, and movement pulls their attention away. Good training starts where your dog can succeed.

Begin in a low-distraction area such as your living room, hallway, backyard, driveway, or a quiet sidewalk. These places allow your dog to practice the mechanics of staying near you and following the leash without becoming overwhelmed. Once your dog can do well there, gradually increase difficulty.

Think of distractions like weight at the gym. You do not start with the heaviest load. You build strength over time. If your dog can walk nicely in the house but loses control outside, that does not mean the training failed. It means the environment is too hard right now, and you need to lower the challenge.

You can also improve your setup by choosing the right time of day. If the neighborhood is full of joggers, dogs, and delivery trucks at 5 p.m., train at 7 a.m. instead. If your dog is wild with energy after being indoors all day, do a short play session or sniff break before asking for focused walking.

  • Start where your dog can pay attention to you.
  • Train in short sessions, around 3 to 10 minutes at first.
  • Increase distractions gradually, not all at once.
  • Pick quieter routes while your dog is learning.
  • Set your dog up physically for success with a bathroom break and a little movement beforehand.

Distance is one of your most powerful tools. If your dog cannot focus near another dog, person, or squirrel, move farther away until they can. Training does not require getting close to distractions. In fact, working at a manageable distance is often the fastest way to build calm behavior.

Create a Predictable Training Routine

Dogs learn well through consistency. If every walk looks chaotic and different, progress will be slower. A simple routine helps your dog understand that walking with you is a skill with clear expectations. It also helps you stay calm and organized.

Before each session, prepare your gear and rewards. Clip the leash on before your dog gets overly excited. Take a breath and decide what today’s goal is. Maybe it is five loose-leash steps in the driveway. Maybe it is checking in with you three times on a quiet block. Small goals lead to steady progress.

Keep sessions short enough that both you and your dog can succeed. End while things are still going well. You do not need to turn every outing into a long training event. In fact, a few focused minutes often teach more than a frustrating half hour. Some walks can be training walks, while others can be decompression walks in low-pressure areas where your dog gets more freedom to sniff and explore.

It also helps to have a consistent start cue and reinforcement pattern. For example, you might ask your dog to stand calmly at the door, mark and reward that calmness, then begin walking. During the walk, reward check-ins, loose leash moments, and staying near your side. This gives the dog a clear picture of what works.

If something goes wrong, avoid turning it into a battle. Pulling, lunging, or ignoring you usually means the environment is too difficult, the reward is too weak, or your dog is too aroused to learn. Step back, create distance, lower the challenge, and try again. Training is not about proving a point. It is about making the right behavior easy to practice.

With proper gear, meaningful rewards, and a thoughtful setup, you are no longer hoping your dog will walk nicely. You are teaching the skill in a way your dog can actually understand. This foundation will make the hands-on exercises in the next chapter much more effective.

Key Takeaways

  • Use safe, comfortable gear such as a well-fitted front-clip harness or flat collar with a regular 4 to 6 foot leash.
  • Avoid retractable leashes during training because they encourage tension and reduce clarity.
  • Choose rewards your dog truly values, and increase reward quality in more distracting environments.
  • Mark the correct behavior immediately so your dog understands what earned the reward.
  • Start training in low-distraction areas and increase difficulty gradually.
  • Use distance, timing, and short sessions to keep your dog under threshold and ready to learn.
  • Create a simple, consistent routine so your dog can build strong walking habits step by step.

Chapter 3: Foundation Skills: Focus, Marker Training, and Following Your Lead

Before a dog can walk politely on a loose leash, they need a few core skills that make communication clear and movement together feel rewarding. Loose-leash walking is not just about stopping pulling. It is about teaching your dog that paying attention to you, understanding your feedback, and choosing to move with you leads to good things. In this chapter, you will build the foundation that makes all leash work easier, faster, and less frustrating.

If you skip these basics, walks often become a tug-of-war. If you teach them first, your dog starts to understand a simple message: staying connected to you is worth it. The three skills you will focus on are attention, marker training, and following your lead. These are simple to teach at home, and they transfer directly to the sidewalk, park, and neighborhood walk.

Teach Focus Before You Ask for Walking

A dog who cannot check in with you indoors will struggle to do it outside, where smells, sounds, people, and other dogs compete for their attention. Focus does not mean your dog must stare at you every second. It means your dog can quickly orient to you when asked and offer check-ins on their own. That skill becomes the engine behind loose-leash walking.

Start in a quiet room with a handful of small, soft treats. Say your dog’s name one time in a cheerful voice. The moment they turn their head toward you, mark the behavior and give a treat. Repeat this several times. Your goal is not to repeat their name louder and louder. Your goal is to teach that hearing their name and looking at you always pays.

Once your dog is responding reliably, begin to wait for one second of eye contact before marking and rewarding. Keep sessions short and easy. Five treats, then a break, is enough. You are building a habit, not testing endurance.

You can also teach a dedicated focus cue such as “look” or “watch.” Hold a treat near your face, say the cue, and when your dog looks at your eyes, mark and reward. After a few repetitions, remove the lure and simply use the cue. Reward any effort at first, then gradually reward clearer, quicker responses.

Practice focus in different rooms before trying it in the yard or driveway. If your dog cannot respond in a new environment, that is not stubbornness. It usually means the distraction level is too high. Move farther away from distractions, use better rewards, or return to an easier setting.

  • Say your dog’s name once, then reward the head turn toward you.
  • Build from a quick glance to one or two seconds of eye contact.
  • Use very short sessions, several times a day.
  • Practice in low-distraction places before moving outdoors.
  • Reward voluntary check-ins during everyday life, not just training time.

A powerful habit to build is the spontaneous check-in. Stand quietly with your dog near you. Wait. The moment your dog looks at you on their own, mark and reward. This teaches your dog that choosing to reconnect with you is valuable. Later, on walks, those spontaneous check-ins become moments when your dog slows down, reorients, and stays close without constant reminders.

Use Marker Training to Make Communication Clear

Marker training is one of the fastest ways to help a dog understand exactly what earned the reward. A marker is a signal that tells your dog, “Yes, that is the behavior I wanted.” It can be a clicker or a short word such as “yes.” The marker bridges the gap between the behavior and the reward, making training more precise.

To start, charge the marker. Say “yes” or click, then immediately give a treat. Do this 10 to 15 times in a row. Your dog does not need to do anything yet. You are simply teaching that the marker predicts a reward. After a few short sessions, your dog will begin to understand that the marker has meaning.

Once the marker is charged, use it to capture behaviors you like. If your dog looks at you, mark and reward. If they step toward your side, mark and reward. If they pause instead of forging ahead, mark and reward. Timing matters. The marker should happen at the exact moment your dog does the correct behavior, not after they have already moved on.

For leash work, marker training is especially useful because walking is dynamic. Many good choices happen quickly. Your dog glances up at you, slows down, returns to your side, or follows when you turn. Marking those moments helps your dog understand what part of the movement earned reinforcement.

Be consistent with your marker. If you say “yes,” make it short and upbeat every time. If you use a clicker, click once per correct behavior. After the marker, always follow with a reward during the learning stage. If the marker does not reliably predict something good, it loses its power.

  • Choose one marker word or a clicker.
  • Charge it by pairing the marker with treats repeatedly.
  • Mark the exact moment your dog makes a good choice.
  • Reward after every marker while teaching new skills.
  • Keep treats small so you can reward often without overfeeding.

A common mistake is waiting too long to mark. If your dog looks at you, then looks away, and only then hears “yes,” they may not know what was rewarded. Another mistake is using the marker as a cue. The marker is not a command. It is information about something your dog already did correctly.

Teach Your Dog to Follow Your Lead

Now that your dog is learning to focus and understands the marker, you can begin teaching them that moving with you is rewarding. This is the beginning of loose-leash walking, but without the pressure of a full walk. Think of it as a simple game of orientation and movement together.

Begin indoors or in a fenced yard. Stand with your dog near you and a few treats ready. Take one step backward or sideways. When your dog moves toward you, mark and reward. Repeat until your dog starts to follow your movement more naturally. Then take two or three steps before marking and rewarding.

Change directions often. Walk a few steps, then turn and go the other way. When your dog notices and follows, mark and reward. This teaches your dog to pay attention to your body and to stay connected rather than charging forward in a straight line. Dogs learn quickly that following your lead is how the game continues.

As your dog improves, reward in the position you want. If you want your dog generally near your left or right side, deliver the treat there. Placement matters. If you always toss the treat ahead, you may accidentally encourage forging. If you reward near your leg, you reinforce the location you want your dog to return to.

You do not need perfect heel position for everyday loose-leash walking. What you want is a dog who stays within a comfortable range, notices your changes in direction, and keeps the leash slack. Keep your criteria realistic. Reward proximity, responsiveness, and a loose leash rather than demanding military precision.

Try this simple following game for 3 to 5 minutes:

  • Start in a quiet area with your dog on leash or off leash in a safe space.
  • Take one to three steps in any direction.
  • When your dog follows and comes into your zone, mark and reward.
  • Turn often so your dog learns to watch you.
  • Pause occasionally and reward your dog for checking in.

If your dog races ahead, do not drag them back. Simply stop, wait for any slack in the leash or any turn back toward you, mark that moment, and reward near your side. This teaches that tension does not move the walk forward, but reconnecting does. Keep your tone calm and your movements clear.

Build These Skills Into Daily Life

The fastest progress happens when training is woven into ordinary routines. You do not need one long session each day. You need many small moments of practice. Ask for focus before meals, at doorways, before tossing a toy, or before clipping on the leash. Reward your dog for following you from room to room. Mark check-ins in the yard. These repetitions create habits that show up on walks.

Remember to train at the right difficulty level. If your dog can focus in the kitchen but not on the front sidewalk, the sidewalk is too hard right now. Reduce the challenge. Work near the door with it open, then on the porch, then a few steps from home. Gradual progress prevents frustration and helps your dog succeed.

Use rewards your dog truly values. In easy environments, kibble may be enough. Outside, you may need chicken, cheese, or another high-value treat. Some dogs also enjoy praise, toys, or permission to sniff as rewards. The more distracting the environment, the more valuable the reinforcement should be.

Keep sessions short, upbeat, and successful. End while your dog is still engaged. If you notice your dog becoming scattered, overexcited, or frustrated, simplify the exercise. Good training is not about pushing through. It is about setting up enough success that your dog wants to keep trying.

These foundation skills may seem basic, but they are the reason some dogs make rapid progress on leash while others struggle. A dog who can focus, understands a marker, and has practiced following your movement is already learning the heart of loose-leash walking: pay attention, stay connected, and good things happen.

Key Takeaways

  • Teach focus first by rewarding your dog for looking at you when they hear their name or cue.
  • Build spontaneous check-ins by marking and rewarding any time your dog chooses to look at you.
  • Use a marker word or clicker to clearly identify the exact behavior you want.
  • Charge the marker before using it in training, and always follow it with a reward while teaching.
  • Practice following games in low-distraction areas so your dog learns to move with you.
  • Reward near your side to encourage the position you want during walks.
  • Keep sessions short, fun, and easy enough for your dog to succeed.
  • Use these skills throughout daily life so they become habits before you expect them on real walks.

Chapter 4: The 14-Day Loose-Leash Training Plan

A loose-leash walk is not built in a single perfect session. It is built through short, clear, repeatable practice that helps your dog understand one simple idea: staying near you makes good things happen, and pulling does not move the walk forward. This 14-day plan is designed to help you make steady progress without overwhelming your dog or yourself.

The goal of this chapter is not perfection by Day 14. The goal is a strong foundation. Some dogs will move quickly, while others will need extra days at certain steps. That is normal. Age, breed, energy level, environment, past habits, and your own timing all affect progress. What matters most is consistency.

Before you begin, gather a few essentials: a comfortable flat collar or front-clip harness, a standard 4- to 6-foot leash, soft high-value treats, and a calm mindset. Keep sessions short, ideally 5 to 10 minutes, and stop while your dog is still successful. If your dog struggles, make the exercise easier rather than pushing through frustration.

How to Use This Plan Successfully

Each day in this plan builds on the previous one. If your dog is having trouble, repeat a day before moving on. Think of this as a flexible roadmap, not a rigid test. You are teaching a skill, not forcing compliance.

  • Train before your dog is overly excited, if possible.
  • Use rewards your dog truly values, especially in distracting environments.
  • Mark the exact behavior you want with a cheerful “Yes!” or a clicker.
  • Reward near your leg or the position where you want your dog to walk.
  • If the leash tightens, stop moving. When the leash loosens, continue.
  • Keep your body language relaxed and your leash hand steady.

Remember that sniffing, exploring, and movement are rewarding to dogs. You can use those natural rewards in training. A loose leash can earn your dog permission to move forward, greet a safe area, or investigate a scent patch. This makes the lesson more meaningful than treats alone.

Days 1-5: Build the Foundation Indoors and in Quiet Spaces

Day 1: Introduce the walking zone. Start indoors with minimal distractions. Stand still with your dog on leash. The moment your dog is beside you and the leash is slack, mark and reward. Take one step. If your dog stays with you, mark and reward again. Focus on tiny wins. Your dog is learning that being near you pays.

Day 2: Add movement and direction changes. Walk a few steps around the room. Mark and reward every couple of steps when the leash stays loose. Randomly turn left, right, or pivot away. When your dog follows and the leash remains slack, reward generously. Direction changes teach your dog to pay attention to your movement.

Day 3: Practice the stop-and-go rule. Move into a hallway, yard, or another quiet area. Walk forward. If your dog pulls, stop immediately. Say nothing or keep it minimal. Wait for any slack in the leash, then mark and continue. This teaches that pulling stalls progress, while a loose leash restarts the walk.

Day 4: Introduce a cue. Begin using a cue such as “Let’s go” before you start moving. Say the cue, take a step, and reward your dog for joining you on a loose leash. Keep sessions upbeat. You are pairing the cue with movement and attention.

Day 5: Build duration in a low-distraction area. Practice in your driveway, hallway, patio, or quiet sidewalk. Ask for 5 to 10 loose-leash steps before rewarding. If that is too hard, reduce the number. The idea is gradual progress. End with a success, even if it is just three good steps.

Days 6-10: Move Outdoors and Add Real-World Challenges

Day 6: Start outside at quiet times. Choose a low-traffic area and a calm time of day. Reward often. Outdoor environments are much harder because smells, sounds, and sights compete for your dog’s attention. Lower your expectations and increase your rate of reinforcement.

Day 7: Use the environment as a reward. If your dog walks nicely toward a tree or grassy patch, allow a sniff break as the reward. You can say, “Go sniff,” and let your dog investigate. This teaches that polite walking leads to freedom and fun.

Day 8: Practice check-ins. During the walk, mark and reward any time your dog looks back at you voluntarily. You are strengthening attention without nagging. Dogs that check in naturally are easier to guide through distractions.

Day 9: Add planned turns and pace changes. Speed up for a few steps, then slow down. Turn away before your dog reaches the end of the leash. Reward your dog for adjusting with you. This helps prevent “autopilot pulling” and keeps the walk interactive.

Day 10: Work around mild distractions. Practice at a distance from small distractions such as parked cars, a person across the street, or a distant dog. The key is distance. If your dog is too excited to take treats or repeatedly hits the end of the leash, you are too close. Move farther away until your dog can succeed.

Days 11-14: Strengthen Reliability and Prepare for Everyday Walks

Day 11: Increase the challenge gradually. Walk a slightly longer route or spend more time in a moderately distracting area. Continue rewarding frequently when your dog makes good choices. If your dog regresses, that is feedback, not failure. Adjust the environment and make it easier.

Day 12: Practice recovery after mistakes. Real walks are imperfect. Today, focus on what happens after pulling. The moment the leash tightens, stop. Wait for slack or encourage your dog back with a kissy noise or by stepping backward. The instant the leash loosens, mark and continue. Fast, calm recovery matters more than never making mistakes.

Day 13: Blend training into a normal walk. Instead of a dedicated drill session, take a regular walk and use your skills naturally. Reward the first 5 minutes heavily, then begin spacing rewards out as your dog succeeds. Continue using sniff breaks, praise, and forward movement as rewards.

Day 14: Test, review, and reset goals. Walk in a familiar but slightly challenging environment. Notice what has improved. Can your dog recover faster? Stay with you longer? Check in more often? Celebrate progress. Then decide what needs more work, such as passing dogs, maintaining focus at the start of the walk, or reducing pulling near favorite spots.

By Day 14, many dogs show clear improvement, but loose-leash walking remains a skill that needs maintenance. Continue practicing several times each week. Reward good choices often enough that your dog stays motivated. Over time, you can reduce food rewards and rely more on praise, sniffing opportunities, and life rewards.

Troubleshooting Common Problems During the 14 Days

My dog pulls harder outside than indoors. This is extremely common. The environment is more rewarding than your living room. Go back to shorter sessions, use better treats, and choose quieter locations. Success outside starts with easier outdoor setups.

My dog ignores treats. Your dog may be too excited, too stressed, or not hungry enough for the reward you are offering. Try higher-value treats like chicken or cheese, train before meals, and increase distance from distractions.

My dog forges ahead at the start of the walk. The beginning of the walk is often the hardest part because anticipation is high. Spend 1 to 2 minutes practicing near the door or driveway before heading off. Reward heavily for calm starts.

My dog zigzags and tangles the leash. Slow down. Reward at your side more often. Use smooth turns and avoid long stretches of wandering until your dog understands the walking zone better.

Progress seems inconsistent. That is normal. Dogs do not learn in a perfectly straight line. One great day may be followed by a messy one. Look for overall improvement across the week, not perfection in every session.

If you stay patient and consistent, your dog will begin to understand that walking with you is a cooperative activity. Loose-leash walking is not about controlling every step. It is about communication, rhythm, and making the right choice easier for your dog than the wrong one.

How to Handle Pulling, Lunging, Distractions, and Setbacks

Loose-leash walking is rarely a straight line from chaos to calm. Most dogs improve in waves: a few good walks, then a rough one; a calm block, then a sudden lunge at a squirrel; a week of progress, then a setback after a stressful day. This is normal. The goal is not perfection. The goal is to build habits that help your dog recover quickly, stay connected to you, and make better choices more often.

In this chapter, you will learn how to respond when your dog pulls, lunges, loses focus, or seems to forget everything you have taught. The key is to avoid turning these moments into a battle. Instead, use them as information. Pulling tells you your dog is overexcited, under-practiced, or moving too fast for their current skill level. Lunging tells you the trigger is too close or too intense. Distractions tell you the environment is harder than expected. Setbacks tell you that training needs to be simplified, not abandoned.

What to Do the Moment Your Dog Starts Pulling

When your dog pulls, the most important rule is simple: do not let pulling be the thing that gets them where they want to go. If pulling works, your dog will repeat it. If pulling consistently pauses progress, your dog can begin to learn that a loose leash is what makes the walk continue.

That does not mean yanking the leash, scolding, or dragging your dog back into place. It means calmly interrupting the pattern and showing your dog a clearer option.

  • Stop moving. The instant the leash goes tight, become still. Plant your feet and wait quietly.
  • Say nothing or use a calm cue. You can wait silently or use a neutral reminder like “easy” or your dog’s name.
  • Reward the release of tension. The moment your dog looks back, steps toward you, or loosens the leash, mark it with praise or a food reward.
  • Move again only on a loose leash. Your dog learns that slack leash makes the walk continue.

If your dog forges ahead repeatedly, use a direction change. Turn and walk a few steps the other way, encouraging your dog to follow. Reward when they catch up and walk near you. This keeps you from getting stuck in a tug-of-war and helps your dog pay attention to your movement.

Practical tip: reward often in the first few minutes of the walk. Many dogs pull hardest at the start because they are excited. If you wait to reward only after your dog is already calm, you may miss the chance to shape better choices early. Feed near your leg, where you want your dog to be. Position matters.

If pulling becomes constant, ask yourself these questions:

  • Is the walk too long for my dog’s current skill level?
  • Am I expecting focus in an environment that is too stimulating?
  • Have I practiced enough indoors or in quiet areas first?
  • Is my dog getting enough exercise, sniffing time, and rest outside of leash training?

Often, the solution is not “be stricter.” It is “make the task easier and reward more clearly.”

How to Handle Lunging Without Escalating It

Lunging can happen toward dogs, people, bikes, cars, wildlife, or exciting smells. Sometimes it comes from frustration and excitement. Sometimes it comes from fear, uncertainty, or overstimulation. Whatever the cause, your first job is management: prevent your dog from rehearsing the lunge as much as possible.

The most effective strategy is distance. If your dog can stay aware of a trigger without exploding, you can train. If your dog is already barking, hitting the end of the leash, or unable to take food, the trigger is too close. Create space immediately.

  • Cross the street when you see a trigger ahead.
  • Step behind a parked car or hedge to block the view.
  • Turn and retreat before your dog reaches the tipping point.
  • Use a cheerful “let’s go” and move away briskly, rewarding your dog for following.

Do not wait until your dog is fully reacting. Learn to spot the early signs: staring, body stiffening, closed mouth, ears forward, weight shifting ahead, tail rising, or ignoring treats. Those signs mean your dog is approaching their threshold. Act early.

Once you have enough distance, begin teaching a replacement behavior. Good options include:

  • Looking at you when they notice the trigger
  • Turning with you on cue
  • Walking a few steps in a hand target
  • Sniffing for tossed treats on the ground

A simple pattern works well: your dog sees the trigger, you mark calmly, and you deliver a treat. Repeat while the trigger is visible and your dog remains under threshold. Over time, your dog begins to associate the trigger with checking in rather than charging forward.

If your dog does lunge, keep safety first. Hold the leash securely, avoid jerking back in panic, and move away as smoothly as you can. Once your dog calms, do not resume the same approach as if nothing happened. Increase distance and lower the difficulty. A successful recovery matters more than “proving” your dog can handle the situation.

Managing Distractions in the Real World

Distractions are not mistakes. They are part of the training plan. Every tree, smell, passing jogger, barking dog, and blowing leaf is a chance to teach your dog how to stay connected while still being a dog. The trick is to control the level of challenge.

Think of distractions in layers. Start with low-level distractions your dog can notice without losing their mind. Then slowly increase difficulty. If you jump from the living room to a busy park, your dog is likely to fail not because they are stubborn, but because the gap is too large.

Use this progression:

  • Practice indoors with no distractions
  • Move to the yard or driveway
  • Train on a quiet street
  • Add mild movement, like one passing person
  • Work near busier environments from a distance
  • Gradually close the distance only as success improves

On walks, remember that not every moment needs formal heelwork. Loose-leash walking is not the same as your dog glued to your leg for thirty minutes. Give your dog structured freedom. Use periods of focused walking, then release them to sniff on a loose leash. Sniffing lowers arousal for many dogs and makes the walk more rewarding, which can reduce frantic pulling.

You can also use high-value rewards strategically. Dry kibble may work in the hallway, but outside you may need chicken, cheese, or another favorite treat. Match the reward to the difficulty. The environment is competing with you, so your reinforcement has to matter.

If your dog becomes distracted easily, shorten your sessions. Five good minutes are better than twenty frustrating ones. End while your dog is still successful. Frequent small wins build reliability faster than long sessions full of mistakes.

How to Recover From Setbacks and Keep Progress Going

Every dog has off days. Weather changes, lack of sleep, a stressful encounter, adolescence, pain, excitement, and routine disruptions can all affect leash behavior. A setback does not erase progress. It simply means your dog needs support at their current level.

When a walk goes badly, resist the urge to label your dog as stubborn or dominant. Instead, review the situation like a trainer:

  • What triggered the pulling or lunging?
  • How close was the trigger?
  • Was my dog already overexcited before we started?
  • Did I bring rewards that were valuable enough?
  • Did I ask for too much for too long?

Then make one or two adjustments for the next walk. Keep it simple. You might choose a quieter route, walk at a calmer time of day, increase distance from triggers, bring better treats, or shorten the session. Improvement comes from these small course corrections.

It also helps to track progress in a basic training log. Write down what went well, what was hard, and what your dog could handle successfully. Patterns will appear. You may notice your dog struggles more in the evening, near other dogs, or after missing exercise. Once you see the pattern, you can plan better.

If your dog is having frequent intense reactions, do not hesitate to slow down and revisit foundations. Practice attention games at home. Reward check-ins in the yard. Rebuild the habit of following your movement. Strong basics make outdoor challenges easier.

Finally, be fair to your dog and to yourself. Training is a skill for both ends of the leash. Your timing will improve. Your observation will improve. Your dog’s ability to recover will improve. Consistency matters more than intensity. Calm, clear repetition is what changes behavior.

If you ever feel stuck, prioritize safety and management while you continue training. Use more distance, choose lower-traffic routes, and avoid known trouble spots when possible. Success is not measured by how close you can get to every distraction. Success is measured by how well your dog can stay engaged and move with you without tension.

Key Takeaways

  • When the leash goes tight, stop moving and reward your dog the moment they create slack.
  • Use direction changes and frequent rewards to prevent pulling from becoming a habit.
  • For lunging, act early and create distance before your dog goes over threshold.
  • Teach replacement behaviors like checking in, turning away, or following a hand target.
  • Increase distractions gradually instead of expecting success in overly difficult environments.
  • Allow sniffing and use short, successful sessions to reduce frustration and build focus.
  • Setbacks are normal; review what happened, adjust the plan, and return to easier wins.

Leash Manners for Puppies, Adult Dogs, and High-Energy Breeds

Loose leash walking is not a one-size-fits-all skill. A young puppy, a recently adopted adult dog, and a high-energy breed may all pull for different reasons, learn at different speeds, and need different kinds of support. The good news is that the core principles stay the same: reward the position you want, prevent rehearsal of pulling, and keep training clear, calm, and consistent.

In this chapter, you will learn how to adjust your leash training plan to match your dog’s age, experience, and energy level. When you tailor your approach, progress becomes faster and less frustrating for both of you. The goal is not a perfectly robotic heel at all times. The goal is a dog who can move through the world with you politely, safely, and with growing self-control.

Training Puppies: Build Habits Before Pulling Starts

Puppies are learning everything at once. The leash, the collar or harness, the outside world, new smells, people, cars, and other dogs can all feel exciting or overwhelming. That is why puppy leash training should focus less on distance and more on creating positive associations and simple habits.

Start indoors or in a quiet yard. Let your puppy wear the harness and drag a lightweight leash for short supervised periods so it becomes normal. Then begin moving together for just a few steps at a time. Reward your puppy often for being near you, looking at you, or following your movement. At this stage, frequent rewards are not bribery; they are communication.

Puppies do best with very short sessions. A few minutes of success is far more valuable than a long walk that turns into overstimulation and pulling. Think in terms of mini-lessons throughout the day rather than one big training event.

  • Reward your puppy for choosing your side before tension appears on the leash.
  • Use a cheerful voice and change direction often to keep your puppy tuned in to you.
  • Stop moving if the leash goes tight, then reward when your puppy returns to you.
  • Practice in low-distraction areas before expecting success on busy sidewalks.
  • Keep walks age-appropriate; young puppies tire quickly and can become mouthy or wild when overtired.

One common mistake with puppies is moving too fast into distracting environments. If your puppy can walk nicely in the kitchen but pulls outside, that does not mean your puppy is stubborn. It means the environment is too difficult. Lower the challenge by choosing a quieter location, increasing distance from distractions, or using more frequent rewards.

Another important point is to avoid constant leash corrections with puppies. You are teaching, not punishing. Gentle guidance, stopping when the leash tightens, and rewarding the right choice will produce better long-term manners than repeated jerks on the leash.

Training Adult Dogs: Replace Old Habits with Clear New Rules

Adult dogs often come to leash training with a history. Some have learned that pulling gets them where they want to go. Others have never been taught what a loose leash means. Rescue dogs may also be adjusting to a new home, new routine, and new trust in their handler. For adult dogs, success depends on consistency and management.

The first step is to stop rewarding pulling. If your dog pulls and you keep walking toward the tree, the grass patch, or the greeting, pulling works. To change the habit, your dog must learn that a tight leash slows or stops progress, while a loose leash makes the walk continue.

Choose one simple rule and stick to it. For example: when the leash tightens, stop. When your dog turns back or steps toward you and loosens the leash, mark the moment with praise or a click and continue walking. This teaches your dog that staying connected to you is what makes the walk move forward.

Adult dogs also benefit from pattern games and predictable exercises. These reduce confusion and help dogs settle into the rhythm of the walk.

  • Practice “walk, stop, reward” in short stretches.
  • Reward check-ins, even if your dog offers only a quick glance.
  • Use changes of direction to prevent your dog from locking onto a destination.
  • Reinforce calm behavior before greetings, sniffing, or crossing the street.
  • End sessions while your dog is still successful rather than after frustration builds.

If your adult dog is strong or highly practiced at pulling, equipment can help with management. A well-fitted front-clip harness may give you more control while you train. Equipment is not the training itself, but it can make it easier to prevent rehearsing the old behavior. Avoid relying on force-based tools as a shortcut. Lasting leash manners come from teaching the dog what to do, not only suppressing what not to do.

Be realistic about your starting point. An adult dog with years of pulling will not usually transform in two perfect walks. But with steady practice, many dogs improve quickly once the rules become predictable and reinforcement becomes meaningful.

High-Energy Breeds: Meet the Need Before You Teach the Skill

High-energy breeds often struggle on leash not because they are disobedient, but because their bodies and brains are primed for action. Sporting breeds, herding breeds, working breeds, and many mixes may find a slow, controlled walk especially difficult if they have excess energy or no outlet for natural behaviors. In these cases, leash training improves dramatically when you first address the dog’s daily needs.

A dog who has had no chance to run, sniff, think, chew, or train is much more likely to hit the end of the leash with full force. Before your formal leash session, consider giving your dog a short activity that takes the edge off without creating more arousal. This might be a sniffing game in the yard, a few minutes of obedience practice, food puzzles, or controlled fetch for dogs who can stay regulated afterward.

For high-energy dogs, mental work is often as important as physical exercise. A dog who learns to pause, orient to the handler, and switch between excitement and calm is developing the exact skills needed for good leash manners.

  • Do a 5- to 10-minute warm-up before the walk, such as hand targets, sits, or scatter feeding.
  • Use high-value rewards in distracting areas; kibble may not compete with birds, joggers, or scents.
  • Allow structured sniff breaks as a reward for loose leash walking.
  • Train in intervals: 30 to 60 seconds of focus, then a release to sniff or explore.
  • Keep your own pace purposeful; drifting slowly can make energetic dogs more frantic.

It also helps to define what kind of walk you are having. Is this a training walk, an exercise walk, or a decompression walk? Trying to do all three at once can create frustration. On a training walk, you may work in short loops near home with lots of reinforcement. On an exercise outing, you may use management and movement to help your dog burn energy safely. On a decompression walk, you may choose a long line in a safe area where your dog can sniff more freely. Clear expectations create better behavior.

For breeds that become overstimulated quickly, watch for the moment your dog tips from excited into out of control. Pulling often starts before the explosion. You may see faster breathing, intense staring, whining, bouncing, or a sudden inability to take treats. When you notice these signs, create distance, lower the difficulty, and ask for something simple your dog can succeed with.

Adjusting Your Plan for Real-Life Success

No matter your dog’s age or breed, progress depends on matching the challenge to your dog’s current skill level. If your dog can walk nicely for ten steps, train for eight. If your dog can handle one passing person but not a crowd, work with one person at a distance. Small wins build reliable habits.

Create a weekly plan instead of hoping each walk will magically improve. For example, spend two days practicing in the driveway, two days on a quiet street, and one day near mild distractions. Keep notes on what caused pulling, what rewards worked best, and how long your dog stayed successful. Patterns will appear quickly, and those patterns tell you what to train next.

Remember that leash manners are influenced by more than training mechanics. Sleep, routine, health, weather, fear, and excitement all matter. A puppy in a fear period, an adult dog with discomfort, or a high-energy dog after a missed exercise day may struggle more than usual. Good trainers do not just repeat the same plan harder. They adjust.

Finally, protect your progress. Do not let your dog spend weekends dragging different family members down the street while you try to teach loose leash skills during the week. Make sure everyone handling the dog understands the same rules: reward proximity, stop or redirect when the leash tightens, and avoid rushing the dog into situations beyond their ability.

Loose leash walking becomes easier when your dog understands that being near you is rewarding, predictable, and worth paying attention to. Whether you are starting with a tiny puppy, retraining an adult, or guiding a powerful high-energy breed, your consistency is what turns practice into habit.

Chapter 7: Making Good Walks Last: Real-World Practice and Long-Term Maintenance

You have done the hard part: you taught your dog what a loose leash means, practiced attention, rewarded good choices, and worked through the first stages of distraction. Now comes the part that turns a training project into a lifestyle. The goal of this chapter is simple: help you keep your dog walking well not just for a few days, but for months and years in the real world.

Loose-leash walking is not a one-time lesson. It is a skill that improves with repetition, thoughtful management, and regular refreshers. Even dogs that walk beautifully can struggle in new environments, during exciting seasons, or after a break in routine. That is normal. Maintenance is not a sign that training failed. Maintenance is how training stays strong.

In this final chapter, you will learn how to practice in everyday situations, handle setbacks without frustration, gradually reduce treats without losing results, and build a long-term plan that keeps walks calm, enjoyable, and realistic.

Practice in the Real World, One Layer at a Time

Your dog may walk nicely in the driveway or on a quiet street, but the real test happens when life gets busy. Other dogs appear, children run past, squirrels dart across the sidewalk, and exciting smells cover every patch of grass. To succeed in these situations, think in terms of layers. Do not jump from easy practice to the hardest possible walk. Add difficulty gradually.

Real-world practice works best when you choose one challenge at a time. If your dog is learning to stay calm around barking dogs, do not also expect perfect walking through a crowded market. If your dog is working on ignoring wildlife, start in an area where you can create distance instead of walking directly into the most tempting environment.

Use a progression like this:

  • Practice in familiar, low-distraction places first
  • Add mild distractions, such as a quiet park or a street with occasional people
  • Work near moderate distractions at a distance your dog can handle
  • Gradually reduce distance or increase duration only when your dog is successful
  • Mix easy walks with harder ones so your dog does not become overwhelmed

When you enter a more distracting area, lower your expectations for a few minutes. Give your dog time to look, sniff, and settle. Reward heavily for check-ins, loose leash moments, and choosing to stay with you. If the leash tightens often, that is a sign the environment may be too difficult right now. Move farther away, slow down, or return to a simpler route.

Remember that successful walks are not measured by distance covered. A ten-minute walk with a loose leash and good engagement is more valuable than a forty-minute struggle full of pulling. Quality matters more than mileage.

Create a Maintenance Routine That Fits Everyday Life

The best long-term training plan is the one you can actually follow. You do not need to run formal drills every day forever, but you do need to keep reinforcing the behaviors you want. A few minutes of intentional practice built into normal walks can maintain excellent leash manners.

Think of maintenance as small, regular investments. Reward your dog for staying near you. Occasionally change direction and praise your dog for following. Ask for a check-in before crossing the street. Pause when the leash gets tight and resume when it softens. These simple habits keep the rules clear.

A practical weekly maintenance routine might include:

  • Two or three walks where you intentionally practice attention and position for 5 to 10 minutes
  • One easier “decompression” walk where sniffing and exploring are allowed on a long line or relaxed leash in a safe area
  • Brief reward refreshers for good walking during normal neighborhood outings
  • One short session in a slightly more distracting environment to keep skills flexible

It also helps to keep your cues consistent. If you use a phrase such as “let’s go” to move forward or “this way” to turn, continue using those same words. Dogs learn patterns quickly, and consistency reduces confusion.

Do not underestimate the value of pre-walk routines. If your dog explodes with excitement the moment the leash appears, spend a minute reinforcing calm before you head out. Ask for a sit, wait for four paws on the floor, or clip the leash on only when your dog is still. The walk begins before you open the door.

Many owners also benefit from setting “walk goals” that are based on behavior, not distance. For example:

  • “Today I will reward ten check-ins.”
  • “Today we will practice calm walking past three driveways.”
  • “Today I will turn away early if I see a trigger before my dog gets too excited.”

These goals make training feel achievable and keep your focus on good decisions rather than perfection.

Reduce Rewards Carefully Without Losing the Behavior

One of the most common questions in dog training is when to stop using treats. The short answer is: do not rush. Rewards are not a crutch. They are part of how behavior stays strong. Over time, you can make rewards less predictable and more varied, but you should still pay your dog for good work.

Instead of eliminating treats suddenly, shift to a smarter reward system. Start by rewarding the best moments: choosing to stay near you when something exciting appears, checking in without being asked, walking nicely through a difficult section, or recovering quickly after distraction. This is called variable reinforcement, and it helps build durable behavior.

You can also use real-life rewards. For many dogs, moving forward, sniffing a bush, greeting a person, or reaching a favorite patch of grass is rewarding. Ask for a moment of loose leash or attention first, then allow access to the thing your dog wants. This teaches your dog that polite walking opens the door to enjoyable experiences.

Good rewards to rotate include:

  • Food treats of different values depending on the environment
  • Verbal praise and cheerful encouragement
  • Permission to sniff
  • Access to a favorite route or grassy area
  • A brief play break, if your dog enjoys toys outdoors

That said, keep high-value treats available for hard situations. If you know your dog struggles near other dogs, busy intersections, or wildlife, bring better rewards. Maintenance does not mean using less support when your dog needs more.

Handle Setbacks Calmly and Keep Progress Moving

Every dog has setbacks. Weather changes behavior. Adolescence changes behavior. A move to a new neighborhood changes behavior. Even a few missed walks or a stressful event can make your dog more reactive, distracted, or pull harder than usual. Do not treat setbacks as failure. Treat them as information.

If your dog starts pulling more again, ask yourself:

  • Has the environment become more difficult?
  • Am I asking for too much too soon?
  • Have I reduced rewards too quickly?
  • Is my dog getting enough rest, enrichment, and exercise outside the walk?
  • Could pain, discomfort, or health issues be affecting behavior?

When problems show up, go back to a level where your dog can succeed. Shorten the walk. Increase distance from distractions. Bring better treats. Reinforce every good choice for a few days. In most cases, a brief return to easier practice restores progress quickly.

It is also wise to separate training walks from exercise needs when necessary. If your dog is full of energy and cannot focus, a short game in the yard, food puzzle, sniffing activity, or fetch session before the walk may improve success. Loose-leash walking requires mental effort, and an overstimulated dog often struggles to make good choices.

For long-term success, keep your expectations realistic. Your dog does not need to walk in perfect heel position for an entire outing. The goal is a leash that stays mostly loose, a dog that can reorient to you, and a walk that feels cooperative rather than like a tug-of-war. Some days will be better than others, and that is okay.

Finally, keep learning your dog. Notice what times of day are easier, which routes create problems, what distance your dog needs from triggers, and what rewards matter most outside. Training becomes much easier when you stop guessing and start observing patterns.

Good walks do not last because dogs become robots. They last because owners stay clear, fair, observant, and consistent. Keep practicing, keep rewarding, and keep making the right behavior easy. Your dog does not need perfection. Your dog needs guidance, repetition, and a human who knows how to build success one walk at a time.
