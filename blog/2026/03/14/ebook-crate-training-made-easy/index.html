Why Crate Training Works: The Fastest Path to a Secure, Well-Behaved Dog

Crate training is one of the simplest and most effective tools you can use to raise a calm, confident, and well-mannered dog. While some owners worry that a crate may feel restrictive or unkind, the truth is that when introduced properly, a crate becomes a place of safety, rest, and routine. Dogs are naturally drawn to small, enclosed spaces where they can relax without feeling exposed. A crate taps into that instinct and gives your dog a reliable place to settle, sleep, and reset.

The reason crate training works so well is that it supports both canine psychology and practical day-to-day training. It helps your dog learn boundaries without confusion. It reduces overstimulation. It prevents destructive behavior before it becomes a habit. Most importantly, it teaches your dog how to be comfortable alone, which is a critical life skill. Whether you have a brand-new puppy, a rescue dog, or an adult dog who needs more structure, crate training can speed up progress in almost every area of behavior.

Used correctly, a crate is not a punishment. It is a management tool, a resting place, and a foundation for trust. Dogs thrive when they know what is expected of them, and a crate creates that clarity. Instead of having too much freedom too soon, your dog learns to succeed in a controlled environment. That success builds confidence, and confidence leads to better behavior.

Crates Create Security and Reduce Anxiety

Many behavior problems begin when a dog feels uncertain, overstimulated, or unable to settle. A crate helps solve this by offering a quiet, predictable space that belongs entirely to your dog. In a busy household, that matters. Children, visitors, noises, and constant activity can overwhelm even friendly dogs. A crate acts like a bedroom door: it gives your dog a chance to rest without having to stay alert to everything happening around them.

Dogs do not always know how to switch themselves off. Puppies especially can become wild, mouthy, and destructive simply because they are overtired. When a puppy is guided into a crate for a nap, many owners are surprised by how quickly the behavior improves. What looked like stubbornness or hyperactivity was often just exhaustion. A crate makes rest possible, and rest supports learning.

For anxious dogs, the crate can become an anchor. Instead of pacing the house or reacting to every movement, the dog learns that the crate is a calm zone. This is especially helpful during stressful moments such as thunderstorms, guests arriving, mealtimes, or household cleaning. The crate does not remove fear by itself, but it gives your dog a familiar place to regroup.

To build this sense of security:

  • Place the crate in a quiet but not isolated area of the home, such as a living room corner or bedroom.
  • Add a comfortable bed or crate mat if your dog does not chew bedding.
  • Use treats, chew toys, and calm praise to create positive associations.
  • Allow your dog to enter voluntarily whenever possible instead of forcing them inside.
  • Keep early crate sessions short and pleasant so your dog learns that the crate predicts good things.

Crate Training Speeds Up House Training and Prevents Bad Habits

One of the biggest reasons crate training is so effective is that it helps with house training faster than almost any other method. Most dogs naturally avoid soiling the place where they sleep. A properly sized crate uses this instinct to encourage bladder and bowel control. If the crate is not too large, your dog is more likely to hold it until they are taken outside.

This does not mean a dog should be left in a crate too long. Success comes from pairing crate time with a consistent potty schedule. When your dog comes out of the crate, they should go directly outside to the designated toilet area. When they eliminate outdoors, reward them immediately with praise or a treat. Over time, your dog begins to understand exactly where to go and what earns rewards.

Crates also prevent the rehearsal of unwanted behaviors. Dogs learn through repetition. If a puppy is free to roam and chew shoes, steal laundry, scratch doors, or have accidents behind the couch, those behaviors become stronger. Every time the dog practices the behavior, it becomes more familiar and more likely to happen again. A crate interrupts that cycle. It keeps your dog from making poor choices when you cannot supervise closely.

This is why crate training is often called management as much as training. Management prevents mistakes. Training teaches the right behavior. Together, they create progress much faster than correction after the fact.

For best results, follow these practical steps:

  • Choose a crate large enough for your dog to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably, but not so large that one side becomes a bathroom area.
  • Take puppies outside after waking, after eating, after play, and after every crate session.
  • Keep a predictable daily routine so your dog learns when to expect potty breaks, meals, naps, and exercise.
  • Supervise closely during free time and use the crate when supervision is not possible.
  • Never punish accidents after they happen. Clean thoroughly and adjust the schedule instead.

Crates Teach Calmness, Independence, and Good Household Manners

A well-behaved dog is not just a dog who knows commands. It is a dog who can relax, wait patiently, and handle everyday life without chaos. Crate training supports these skills by teaching your dog that they do not need to be involved in every moment. They can rest while you cook dinner. They can stay calm when visitors come over. They can settle during work calls or while the family eats.

This kind of emotional control is valuable. Many dogs struggle not because they are disobedient, but because they have never learned how to be still. A crate gives them a clear structure for practicing that skill. Instead of following you from room to room, barking for attention, or becoming frantic when separated, your dog learns that short periods of quiet independence are normal and safe.

Crate training is also useful in real-life situations beyond the home. Dogs may need to stay in a crate at the veterinarian, groomer, boarding facility, or during travel. A dog who is already comfortable in a crate handles these experiences with less stress. That makes life easier for both the dog and the owner.

To use the crate for teaching manners:

  • Give your dog a stuffed food toy or safe chew in the crate to reward calm behavior.
  • Use the crate during predictable busy times, such as meal prep, guest arrivals, or children's playtime.
  • Practice short departures so your dog learns that you always come back.
  • Release your dog from the crate only when they are calm, not while barking or jumping.
  • Balance crate time with exercise, training, play, and affection so your dog’s needs are fully met.

How to Make Crate Training Work Quickly and Humanely

The key to successful crate training is not simply using a crate. It is using it in a way that builds trust. The fastest path to success comes from making the crate feel rewarding, predictable, and safe. Start by leaving the crate door open and letting your dog explore it at their own pace. Toss treats inside, feed meals near or in the crate, and praise any voluntary interest. Once your dog is comfortable entering, begin closing the door briefly while they enjoy a treat or chew.

Increase duration gradually. A few seconds becomes a minute, then a few minutes, then longer sessions. If your dog becomes distressed, go back a step and rebuild confidence. Progress is usually faster when the dog is slightly tired from a walk or play session before crate time. Calm energy makes learning easier.

It is also important to avoid common mistakes. Do not use the crate as a place your dog only goes when something bad happens. Do not crate for excessive hours without breaks, exercise, and interaction. Do not rush the process and expect instant comfort. A crate should be part of a healthy routine, not a substitute for attention or training.

Think of the crate as a tool that helps your dog practice success. When used thoughtfully, it supports nearly every training goal: house training, chewing prevention, calm behavior, independence, sleep quality, and safety. It gives your dog a dependable place in your home and gives you a practical way to guide behavior without constant conflict. That is why crate training works so well—and why it remains one of the fastest paths to a secure, well-behaved dog.

Key Takeaways

  • A crate provides dogs with a secure, predictable space that encourages calmness and rest.
  • Crate training speeds up house training by supporting natural instincts to keep sleeping areas clean.
  • It prevents destructive habits by limiting unsupervised freedom before bad behaviors can develop.
  • Crates help teach independence, patience, and better household manners.
  • Success comes from positive associations, gradual progress, and using the crate humanely as part of a balanced routine.

Chapter 2: Choosing the Right Crate: Size, Style, Setup, and Must-Have Essentials

Before crate training can feel easy, the crate itself needs to be the right fit for your dog and your home. A well-chosen crate supports comfort, safety, calm behavior, and training success. The wrong crate, on the other hand, can make even the best training plan feel frustrating. Size matters, but so do style, placement, and the items you put inside. In this chapter, you will learn how to choose a crate your dog can relax in, how to set it up correctly, and which essentials truly help.

Think of the crate as your dog’s personal room. It should feel secure, predictable, and pleasant. Whether you are bringing home a new puppy, helping an adult rescue settle in, or simply creating a safe resting space, the goal is the same: make the crate inviting and practical from day one.

Choose the Right Crate Size

One of the most common mistakes owners make is choosing a crate that is too large or too small. The ideal crate gives your dog enough room to stand up without crouching, turn around comfortably, and lie down fully stretched or curled up. If the crate is too tight, your dog may feel trapped and uncomfortable. If it is too roomy, your dog may be more likely to use one end as a sleeping area and the other as a bathroom, especially during house training.

For puppies, this can be tricky because they grow quickly. In many cases, a crate with a divider panel is the best solution. A divider allows you to adjust the usable space as your puppy grows, so you do not need to buy multiple crates in a short period of time. Start with just enough space for sleeping and turning around, then expand gradually.

To estimate the right size, measure your dog carefully:

  • Measure from the tip of the nose to the base of the tail.
  • Measure from the floor to the top of the head or ears, whichever is taller when standing.
  • Add a few inches to each measurement for comfort.

If your dog is between sizes, consider your dog’s sleeping style and body shape. Long-bodied breeds may need extra length. Tall breeds may need more height. Dogs who like to stretch out often appreciate a little more floor space, but avoid going so large that the crate loses its cozy feeling.

Also think about purpose. A crate used mainly for sleeping can be slightly more snug than a crate used for travel or longer rest periods. For house training, appropriate sizing is especially important because it helps encourage bladder control and clean habits.

Pick the Best Crate Style for Your Dog and Lifestyle

There is no single “best” crate for every dog. The right style depends on your dog’s temperament, age, chewing habits, travel needs, and the layout of your home. Each crate type has strengths and drawbacks, so choose based on how you plan to use it.

  • Wire crates: These are popular because they offer excellent airflow and visibility. Many fold flat for storage and include divider panels. They are a great choice for home use, especially for puppies in training.
  • Plastic crates: These feel more enclosed and den-like, which some dogs find comforting. They are often used for travel and can work well for dogs who get overstimulated by too much activity around them.
  • Soft-sided crates: These are lightweight and portable, ideal for calm dogs and short-term use. They are not suitable for dogs who chew, scratch, or panic in confinement.
  • Heavy-duty crates: These are designed for strong, determined escape artists or dogs with a history of damaging standard crates. They are more expensive but can be a worthwhile investment for safety.
  • Furniture-style crates: These are made to blend with home decor. They can be attractive, but be sure they are sturdy, easy to clean, and truly appropriate for your dog’s size and behavior.

If your dog is anxious or highly active, durability and safety should come before appearance. If you travel often, portability may matter most. If your dog is sensitive to noise and movement, a more enclosed crate may help them settle faster.

Pay attention to practical details as well. Look for secure latches, smooth edges, easy-clean trays, and good ventilation. If your dog is a skilled nose-pusher or paw-worker, test the latch carefully. Safety should never be an afterthought.

Set Up the Crate for Comfort and Success

Where you place the crate can strongly affect how your dog feels about it. Most dogs do best when the crate is in a part of the home where they can feel included but not overwhelmed. A quiet corner of the living room, family room, or bedroom often works well. Avoid putting the crate in direct sun, next to a heater, in a drafty hallway, or in a noisy area with constant foot traffic.

For many puppies, keeping the crate in or near your bedroom at night can make the adjustment much easier. Being close to you can reduce stress and help you hear when your puppy needs a nighttime bathroom break. During the day, a second crate or a portable option in the main living area can also be helpful.

Inside the crate, keep the setup simple and comfortable. You do not need to fill it with lots of items. Start with the basics and adjust according to your dog’s behavior.

  • A crate mat, pad, or bed if your dog does not shred bedding
  • A safe chew toy or durable enrichment item
  • A light crate cover if your dog relaxes better with less visual stimulation
  • Easy access to fresh water when appropriate and safe for the situation

Some dogs love plush beds, while others overheat easily or chew fabric. If your dog destroys bedding, remove it and use a more durable mat or no bedding at all until chewing improves. Comfort matters, but safety comes first.

If you use a crate cover, make sure airflow remains good. Some dogs settle beautifully with three sides covered, while others become more anxious if they cannot see out. Watch your dog’s response and adjust. The best setup is the one that helps your dog relax.

Keep the crate clean and odor-free. Wash bedding regularly, wipe down surfaces, and remove crumbs, fur, and accidents promptly. A clean crate is more inviting and supports better house training habits.

Must-Have Essentials and Smart Buying Tips

You do not need to buy every crate accessory on the market. Focus on items that improve safety, comfort, and training value. A few well-chosen essentials can make a big difference.

  • Divider panel: Essential for growing puppies in larger wire crates.
  • Removable tray: Makes cleanup faster and easier after accidents.
  • Comfortable bedding: Choose washable, durable materials suited to your dog’s habits.
  • Safe chew toys: Help your dog associate the crate with calm, rewarding time.
  • Crate cover or blanket: Useful for some dogs, but monitor ventilation and comfort.
  • Non-slip surface under the crate: Helpful on hardwood or tile floors to prevent shifting.
  • Treat pouch nearby: Makes it easier to reward crate entries and calm behavior consistently.

When shopping, resist the urge to buy based on looks alone. Read product reviews with a practical eye. Are owners reporting bent doors, broken latches, rust, weak zippers, or difficult assembly? A stylish crate is not a good value if it fails under normal use.

It is also wise to think ahead. If you have a puppy, ask what the dog’s adult size and energy level are likely to be. If you have a rescue dog, choose a crate that gives you flexibility while you learn more about their preferences. If your dog will ride in a car crate, make sure the model is suitable for travel use and fits your vehicle properly.

Finally, remember that the crate itself does not do the training. A perfect crate will not solve fear, separation distress, or poor introductions. But the right crate creates the conditions for success. It becomes a calm, secure place where your dog can rest, reset, and feel safe.

As you move into the next stage of crate training, your goal is not just to own a crate, but to create an environment your dog wants to enter. Good choices now will make every training session smoother and help your dog build positive associations from the start.

Key Takeaways

  • Choose a crate that allows your dog to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably without giving excessive extra space.
  • Use a divider panel for puppies so the crate can grow with them.
  • Select a crate style based on your dog’s temperament, chewing habits, travel needs, and your home setup.
  • Place the crate in a quiet but connected area where your dog feels included and safe.
  • Keep the crate setup simple, clean, and comfortable with safe bedding and appropriate chew items.
  • Prioritize safety features such as secure latches, ventilation, durability, and easy cleaning.
  • The best crate is one that supports calm behavior, positive associations, and consistent training.

Chapter 3: The First 7 Days: How to Introduce the Crate Without Fear, Whining, or Resistance

The first week of crate training sets the tone for everything that follows. If your dog learns that the crate is safe, predictable, and rewarding, you will avoid many of the common problems owners fear most: panic, nonstop whining, refusal to enter, and stress around being confined. The goal during these first seven days is not to force long periods in the crate. It is to build trust, create positive associations, and teach your dog that the crate is a calm place where good things happen.

Whether you are working with a young puppy or an older rescue dog, the same principle applies: move at your dog’s pace while still being consistent. You are not trying to “win” a battle. You are helping your dog develop a new habit. Short, positive sessions are far more effective than long, stressful ones. In this chapter, you will learn exactly how to introduce the crate step by step over the first seven days so your dog feels secure instead of trapped.

Before Day 1: Set Up the Crate for Success

Before you begin training, make sure the crate itself is working for your dog, not against them. A crate that is too large, too small, too exposed, or placed in a poor location can make training harder than it needs to be. Your dog should be able to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably, but not have excessive extra space if house training is one of your goals.

Place the crate in an area where your dog can feel included. For most homes, that means a living room, kitchen, or bedroom rather than an isolated laundry room or basement. Dogs are social animals. If the crate feels like solitary confinement, resistance is more likely. Add a soft crate mat or blanket if your dog does not chew bedding. Leave the door open and secure it so it does not swing and startle your dog.

To make the crate inviting, prepare a few high-value rewards and enrichment items:

  • Small soft treats your dog loves
  • A stuffed food toy or lick mat
  • A safe chew for quiet crate time
  • A light blanket over part of the crate if your dog relaxes better in a cozy den-like space

Do not begin by shutting your dog inside and hoping they “get used to it.” That approach often creates fear and makes whining worse. Your dog should first learn that entering the crate is their choice and that the crate predicts pleasant experiences.

Days 1-2: Build Positive Associations and Curiosity

During the first two days, your job is simple: help your dog discover that the crate is not scary. Leave the door open and toss treats near the crate, then just inside it, then farther in as your dog becomes comfortable. Let your dog go in and out freely. Praise softly when they step inside. Keep your tone calm and cheerful, not overly excited.

You can also feed meals near the crate and gradually move the bowl closer to the entrance, then just inside the crate. If your dog is hesitant, do not push or lure too aggressively. A dog who feels pressured may become suspicious. Instead, reward any sign of interest, such as looking at the crate, sniffing it, or placing one paw inside.

A simple training session might look like this:

  • Toss one treat just inside the crate
  • Allow your dog to walk in and eat it
  • Let them come back out immediately
  • Repeat 5 to 10 times
  • End the session before your dog loses interest

If your dog is already comfortable entering the crate, begin adding a cue such as “crate,” “bed,” or “kennel.” Say the cue once, toss a treat inside, and praise when your dog enters. Over time, your dog will connect the word with the action.

On these early days, avoid closing the door for more than a second or two, if at all. The focus is confidence, not duration. Several short sessions throughout the day are ideal.

Days 3-5: Introduce Short Closed-Door Sessions Without Drama

Once your dog is willingly entering the crate, eating treats inside, and showing relaxed body language, you can begin very short closed-door sessions. This is where many owners move too quickly. Start with seconds, not minutes.

Give your dog a treat or stuffed toy in the crate, gently close the door, then sit nearby. Before your dog becomes upset, open the door again. If your dog stays calm for 5 seconds, that is a success. Repeat several times. Then gradually extend the time to 10, 15, or 30 seconds over multiple sessions.

Watch your dog’s body language carefully. Signs that you are moving too fast include:

  • Intense whining or barking immediately after the door closes
  • Pawing frantically at the door
  • Refusing treats
  • Heavy panting when the room is cool
  • Trying to escape or biting the crate bars

If you see these signs, shorten the session and go back to easier steps. The crate should feel boring and safe, not emotionally overwhelming.

During days 3 through 5, begin practicing tiny moments of distance. Close the door, give your dog a chew, take one step away, then return and open the door while your dog is still calm. Later, take two or three steps away. Then sit in a nearby chair for a minute. These small progressions teach your dog that you can move away and come back without anything bad happening.

If your dog whines briefly, do not panic. A few seconds of mild protest can be normal, especially if your dog is adjusting to a new routine. Wait for a brief pause in the whining, then calmly open the door. This helps prevent teaching your dog that nonstop noise makes the crate open. However, if whining escalates into panic, the session is too difficult and should be made easier next time.

Days 6-7: Build Routine, Calm Exits, and Real-Life Practice

By the end of the first week, your dog should be entering the crate willingly and staying inside for short, comfortable periods. Now you can begin using the crate in more practical ways, such as during meal prep, while you answer emails, or for a short rest period after playtime. Keep these sessions realistic and manageable. A tired dog with a chew item is much more likely to settle than a dog who has excess energy.

Use a simple rhythm each time:

  • Potty break first
  • Short play or training session
  • Into the crate with a chew, treat, or meal
  • Calm background environment
  • Quiet release when your dog is settled

This sequence works because it meets your dog’s needs in the correct order. Many crate problems happen when owners put a dog into the crate with a full bladder, too much energy, or no mental stimulation. The crate then becomes associated with frustration rather than rest.

Practice calm exits too. Do not open the crate the moment your dog gets excited and starts bouncing. Wait for a moment of relative calm, then open the door quietly. This teaches your dog that calm behavior makes freedom happen. If your dog rushes out wildly, you can gently pause and ask for a brief sit before moving on, if they know that cue.

It is also helpful to vary your presence. Sit nearby for one session, leave the room for 10 seconds in another, then return. Build gradually. The purpose is to prevent your dog from thinking the crate is only safe when you are directly beside them.

Remember that nighttime and daytime crate training can feel different to your dog. Many puppies settle more easily at night when the house is quiet and they are sleepy. Daytime crate sessions often require more deliberate planning and shorter durations at first.

Throughout the week, avoid these common mistakes:

  • Using the crate only when you leave the house
  • Putting the dog in only after they are already overexcited
  • Letting whining continue for long periods without adjusting the plan
  • Using the crate as punishment
  • Expecting too much too soon

Most importantly, keep your emotions steady. Dogs read our tension. If you act guilty, frustrated, or apologetic every time you use the crate, your dog may learn that the crate is something to worry about. Be calm, matter-of-fact, and consistent. The crate is simply part of normal daily life.

At the end of the first seven days, your dog does not need to love long crate stays yet. That is not the standard. Success means your dog is beginning to see the crate as a place where they can enter voluntarily, relax briefly, enjoy rewards, and trust that you will return. That foundation is what makes longer crate training possible in the chapters ahead.

Key Takeaways

  • Use the first week to build trust and positive associations, not long confinement.
  • Keep the crate comfortable, well-placed, and open for exploration at first.
  • Reward curiosity and voluntary entry with treats, meals, praise, and chews.
  • Introduce closed-door sessions gradually, starting with just a few seconds.
  • Increase time and distance slowly while watching for signs of stress.
  • Always set your dog up with a potty break and some activity before crate time.
  • Release your dog when calm so calm behavior becomes the habit.
  • Consistency, patience, and short successful sessions prevent fear and resistance.

Creating a Daily Crate Routine for Puppies, Adult Dogs, Naps, and Bedtime

A crate works best when it becomes part of a predictable daily rhythm rather than a place your dog only enters when you leave the house. Dogs thrive on routine. When meals, potty breaks, playtime, training, naps, and bedtime happen in a familiar pattern, your dog relaxes faster and settles more easily in the crate. A good crate routine helps with house training, prevents overtired behavior, reduces anxiety, and teaches your dog that quiet time is normal and safe.

This chapter will help you build a practical crate schedule for both puppies and adult dogs. You do not need a perfect clock-based plan, but you do need consistency. Think in terms of patterns: potty, activity, rest; potty, activity, rest. Once your dog learns this cycle, crate time becomes much smoother.

Why a Daily Crate Routine Matters

Many crate training problems come from inconsistency. If one day the crate is used for naps, and the next day it is only used when guests arrive or when the dog is being corrected, the crate can start to feel unpredictable. A routine changes that. It turns the crate into a normal part of life.

A structured routine helps in several ways:

  • Supports house training by timing crate periods around potty breaks
  • Prevents overstimulation and overtired puppy behavior
  • Builds independence by teaching your dog to rest alone
  • Creates calmer transitions during work hours, errands, and bedtime
  • Reduces whining because your dog begins to expect what happens next

For puppies, crate routines are especially important because they need frequent potty trips, short bursts of activity, and a lot of sleep. Young puppies often need 18 to 20 hours of sleep in a day. Without scheduled rest, they can become mouthy, wild, and unable to settle. For adult dogs, the crate can still be a valuable tool for rest, safety, travel preparation, and maintaining household structure.

When creating your routine, match crate time to your dog’s age, energy level, and experience. A very young puppy cannot stay crated as long as a mature dog. A newly adopted adult dog may also need shorter, gentler crate sessions while they build confidence.

Building a Crate Schedule for Puppies and Adult Dogs

The simplest routine is to place crate time after your dog’s needs have been met. A dog who has recently gone potty, had some movement, and done a little mental work is much more likely to settle. Avoid putting a restless, under-exercised dog in the crate and expecting immediate calm.

For puppies, use the crate throughout the day for short rest periods. A helpful pattern is:

  • Wake up and go outside immediately
  • Eat, drink, and have supervised play or training
  • Go outside again
  • Settle in the crate for a nap
  • Repeat this cycle throughout the day

Very young puppies may only manage 45 to 90 minutes of awake time before they need help settling for sleep. If your puppy becomes zoomy, nippy, or unable to focus, it is often a sign they need a nap rather than more stimulation.

Adult dogs usually need fewer crate sessions during the day, but the same principle applies. Give them a chance to toilet, move, and decompress before crate time. If you work from home, an adult dog may benefit from one or two planned crate rests so they learn that they do not need to follow you constantly from room to room.

Here is a practical example of how a puppy day might look:

  • 6:30 a.m. - Potty break
  • 6:45 a.m. - Breakfast
  • 7:00 a.m. - Play, short training, supervised exploration
  • 7:45 a.m. - Potty break
  • 8:00 a.m. - Crate nap
  • 10:00 a.m. - Potty break, play, enrichment
  • 11:00 a.m. - Potty break
  • 11:15 a.m. - Crate nap
  • 1:00 p.m. - Potty, lunch if age-appropriate, activity
  • 2:00 p.m. - Potty break
  • 2:15 p.m. - Crate nap
  • 4:00 p.m. - Potty, play, training walk or yard time
  • 5:30 p.m. - Dinner
  • Evening - Potty breaks, calm family time, one more short crate rest if needed
  • Bedtime - Final potty trip, then into the crate for the night

And here is a simple adult dog routine:

  • Morning potty break and walk
  • Breakfast
  • Short calm period or chew in the crate
  • Midday potty and exercise break
  • Afternoon crate rest if needed
  • Evening walk, dinner, family time
  • Final potty break before bed
  • Overnight crate sleep if that is your long-term setup

Keep in mind that schedules should be adjusted gradually. If your dog currently tolerates 20 minutes calmly, do not jump to two hours. Build duration in small, successful steps.

Using the Crate for Naps and Quiet Time

Daytime naps are one of the most useful and overlooked parts of crate training. Many dogs, especially puppies, do not know how to stop and rest on their own. The crate creates a low-distraction environment where sleep is easier.

To make nap time successful:

  • Schedule naps after potty breaks
  • Offer a brief calming activity first, such as a chew or stuffed food toy
  • Use a consistent nap cue like “crate time” or “settle”
  • Keep the area quiet and avoid constant interruptions
  • Do not wait until your puppy is frantic and overtired

Watch your dog’s natural rhythm. If your puppy tends to get wild mid-morning and late afternoon, plan crate naps just before that behavior escalates. Preventing the problem is easier than trying to calm an overstimulated puppy afterward.

For adult dogs, quiet crate time can be used after exercise, while you work, during children’s busy hours, or when visitors come over. The goal is not to isolate the dog unnecessarily, but to teach them how to rest without needing constant engagement.

If your dog fusses briefly before settling, pause and listen before rushing over. Some light movement or a few seconds of whining can simply be part of settling down. If the dog has been exercised, pottied, and introduced to the crate positively, give them a moment to relax. If the fussing escalates, review whether the crate session was too long, mistimed, or introduced too quickly.

Creating a Calm Bedtime Routine

Bedtime crate training is often easier when the evening follows a calm pattern. Dogs do best when the last hour before bed is predictable and low-key. Avoid a late burst of exciting play right before lights out. Instead, think calm walk, final potty, quiet settling, then sleep.

A solid bedtime routine usually includes:

  • Feeding dinner early enough to allow digestion and a potty break before bed
  • A final walk or bathroom trip
  • Reduced noise and activity in the home
  • A consistent bedtime cue
  • A comfortable crate setup with safe bedding if your dog does not chew it

For young puppies, place the crate near your bed at first. This helps them feel less alone and lets you hear when they truly need a nighttime potty break. Many puppies cannot make it through the night immediately, and expecting too much too soon can slow house training. If your puppy wakes, take them out calmly and quietly, then return them to the crate without play or excitement.

As your puppy matures, you can gradually increase the time between nighttime potty trips and, if you choose, slowly move the crate to its long-term location. Adult dogs who are already house trained may sleep through the night from the start, but they still benefit from the same calm bedtime pattern.

Avoid these common bedtime mistakes:

  • Putting the dog to bed without a final potty trip
  • Using the crate only when the dog is in trouble
  • Responding to every sound with attention and conversation
  • Letting the dog fall asleep outside the crate, then moving them after they are fully asleep every night

If you want the crate to be the regular nighttime sleeping space, help your dog fall asleep there consistently. You can make it pleasant with a soft cue, a safe chew for a few minutes, and a calm environment.

Above all, stay realistic and patient. Routine does not mean rigidity. Some days will vary. What matters most is that your dog can predict the general flow of the day and trust that the crate is a normal, comfortable place to rest.

Key Takeaways

  • Use the crate as part of a daily pattern, not only when you leave or when problems happen
  • Schedule crate time after potty breaks, activity, and brief mental stimulation
  • Puppies need frequent naps and often settle better with planned crate rest
  • Adult dogs also benefit from routine quiet time in the crate
  • Keep bedtime calm, consistent, and paired with a final potty trip
  • Adjust crate duration gradually based on your dog’s age, needs, and comfort level

How to Handle Crying, Barking, Separation Anxiety, and Common Crate Training Setbacks

Crate training rarely follows a perfectly straight path. Even dogs that seem comfortable at first may suddenly begin whining, barking, pawing at the door, or resisting the crate altogether. This does not mean you have failed, and it does not mean your dog is “bad.” In most cases, these setbacks are simply part of the learning process. Your job is to stay calm, observe what your dog is communicating, and respond in a way that builds confidence rather than fear.

The most important thing to remember is that not all crate-related crying or barking means the same thing. A puppy who needs a bathroom break, a dog who has too much pent-up energy, and a dog experiencing true panic will all sound upset, but the solution for each situation is different. Successful crate training depends on learning to tell the difference and adjusting your approach accordingly.

Understanding Why Your Dog Is Crying or Barking

Before you can solve a crate training problem, you need to identify the cause. Crying and barking are symptoms, not the root issue. Dogs vocalize in the crate for many common reasons, including boredom, frustration, fear, discomfort, lack of exercise, or simply because they have learned that noise gets your attention.

Ask yourself a few practical questions before reacting:

  • Has your dog recently gone to the bathroom?
  • Has your dog had enough physical exercise and mental stimulation?
  • Is the crate the correct size and comfortable?
  • Is the room too hot, cold, noisy, or overstimulating?
  • Did you increase crate time too quickly?
  • Is your dog tired but unable to settle, or truly distressed?

If your dog is whining for a minute or two after being crated, that can be normal adjustment behavior, especially with puppies. However, prolonged barking, frantic scratching, heavy panting, drooling, biting at the crate, or attempts to escape suggest a more serious issue. In those cases, do not simply “wait it out” indefinitely. A dog in panic is not learning calmness; that dog is learning that the crate feels unsafe.

Keep a simple training log for several days. Write down when the barking happens, how long it lasts, what happened before crate time, and how you responded. Patterns often become clear very quickly. You may discover that your dog cries only when overtired, only when left alone, or only after long periods without exercise.

What to Do in the Moment

When your dog starts crying or barking in the crate, your response matters. The goal is to avoid rewarding attention-seeking noise while still meeting legitimate needs and preventing fear from escalating.

Start with a calm pause. If your dog has just been placed in the crate and begins mild whining, wait briefly before responding. Many dogs settle on their own if given the chance. Do not rush over at the first sound, as that can accidentally teach your dog that vocalizing makes you appear.

At the same time, do not ignore obvious distress. If your dog is escalating, check the basics. A young puppy may genuinely need to go outside. Quietly take your dog out on leash for a brief bathroom break with no play, no excitement, and no reward for barking. If your dog does not eliminate, return them to the crate calmly.

Use these practical steps during setbacks:

  • Wait for a brief pause in barking before opening the crate whenever possible.
  • Keep your voice neutral and calm. Avoid scolding, pleading, or repeated reassurance.
  • Offer a stuffed food toy or safe chew to create a positive association.
  • Cover part of the crate with a light blanket if your dog relaxes better with fewer visual distractions.
  • Move the crate closer to you at night or during early training if distance increases anxiety.
  • Shorten the crate session to a length your dog can handle successfully, then rebuild gradually.

One of the most effective strategies is to return to easier repetitions. If your dog struggles when crated for 30 minutes, practice several successful sessions of 5 to 10 minutes instead. Success builds confidence. Pushing too far too fast often creates bigger setbacks.

Also pay attention to your departures and arrivals. If you make leaving the house feel dramatic, your dog may become more aroused before crate time even begins. Practice calm exits. Pick up your keys, put on your shoes, and move around the house without always leaving. This helps reduce the emotional charge around departure cues.

Handling Separation Anxiety vs. Normal Crate Resistance

It is important to distinguish between a dog who simply dislikes confinement and a dog with separation anxiety. Separation anxiety is a deeper emotional issue centered on being away from a specific person or being left alone. Dogs with separation anxiety may panic in a crate, but the crate itself is not always the true problem.

Common signs of separation anxiety include:

  • Extreme distress when you prepare to leave
  • Persistent barking or howling that does not decrease over time
  • Heavy panting, drooling, or trembling
  • Escape attempts that risk injury
  • Destructive behavior focused on doors, windows, or the crate
  • Accidents in the house or crate despite being house-trained
  • Refusal to eat when left alone

If your dog shows these signs, avoid forcing long crate sessions. For some anxious dogs, a crate can intensify panic rather than soothe it. Instead, work on gradual independence training. Start by teaching your dog to relax while you move short distances away. Step out of sight for one or two seconds, return calmly, and repeat. Build duration slowly and only at a pace your dog can tolerate.

You can also create a broader “safe space” using a puppy-proofed room, exercise pen, or gated area if the crate is too restrictive at this stage. Pair alone time with high-value enrichment such as a frozen food toy, snuffle mat, or long-lasting chew. The goal is to teach your dog that your absence predicts calm, safe, rewarding experiences.

For moderate to severe separation anxiety, professional support is strongly recommended. A qualified trainer experienced in anxiety cases or a veterinary behavior professional can help you create a structured treatment plan. In some cases, medical support may be appropriate. Early intervention prevents the problem from becoming more deeply ingrained.

Preventing and Recovering from Common Setbacks

Setbacks are common during crate training, especially during changes in routine, growth stages, illness, travel, moving homes, or after a frightening event. A dog that was doing well may temporarily regress. When that happens, go back to the foundations rather than trying to overpower the problem.

Here are several common setbacks and how to handle them:

  • Your puppy suddenly hates the crate: Rebuild positive associations with meals in the crate, short toss-in-and-out treat games, and brief relaxed sessions with the door open.
  • Your dog only cries at night: Make sure bedtime includes a bathroom trip, place the crate near your bed, and consider whether your puppy has outgrown their overnight holding ability.
  • Your dog enters the crate but barks when the door closes: Practice door desensitization by closing the door for one second, then opening it before your dog becomes upset. Gradually increase duration.
  • Your dog regressed after a stressful event: Reduce expectations for a few days and focus on short, easy, successful sessions paired with comfort and predictability.
  • Your dog barks for attention: Increase exercise and enrichment, reward quiet behavior, and avoid opening the crate during active barking unless there is a genuine need.

Daily routine plays a huge role in crate success. Dogs settle better when their needs are met consistently. A dog with unspent energy, little mental stimulation, and no predictable schedule is far more likely to protest being crated.

Support better crate behavior with these habits:

  • Provide age-appropriate exercise before longer crate periods.
  • Use food puzzles, stuffed toys, and chew items to encourage calm settling.
  • Keep crate sessions realistic for your dog’s age and experience level.
  • Practice short crate sessions even when you are home, so the crate does not only predict your absence.
  • Reward voluntary crate entry with treats, praise, or meals.
  • Maintain a calm, predictable routine around crate time.

Most importantly, avoid using the crate as punishment. If the crate becomes associated with anger, isolation, or forced confinement after stressful moments, your dog will naturally resist it. The crate should feel like a safe resting place, not a penalty box.

Progress in crate training is often uneven, but consistency wins. Stay observant, adjust your plan based on what your dog is telling you, and celebrate small improvements. A quieter crate session, a faster recovery after barking, or a relaxed body posture are all signs that your dog is learning. With patience and a thoughtful approach, most crate training problems can be improved significantly.

Key Takeaways

  • Identify the reason behind crying or barking before choosing a solution.
  • Mild whining may be normal, but panic behaviors should never be ignored.
  • Meet basic needs first: bathroom, exercise, comfort, and appropriate crate duration.
  • Wait for quiet when possible, but do not let true distress escalate unchecked.
  • Shorten crate time and rebuild gradually after setbacks.
  • Separation anxiety is different from normal crate resistance and may require professional help.
  • Keep the crate positive, predictable, and never use it as punishment.

Chapter 6: Using the Crate for Housebreaking, Travel, Vet Visits, and Everyday Calm Behavior

Once your dog sees the crate as a safe, familiar place, it becomes much more than a training tool. It can support housebreaking, make travel easier, reduce stress during vet visits, and help your dog learn how to settle calmly in daily life. The key is to use the crate thoughtfully. It should never feel like punishment. Instead, it should consistently predict rest, safety, and success.

In this chapter, you will learn how to apply crate training in real-world situations. These practical uses can make life easier for both you and your dog while reinforcing good habits that last well beyond puppyhood.

Using the Crate for Housebreaking Success

One of the most effective uses of a crate is housebreaking. Dogs naturally avoid soiling the area where they sleep, which makes the crate a helpful way to teach bladder and bowel control. When used correctly, the crate helps your dog learn to “hold it” for short periods and creates a predictable routine for potty breaks.

The crate should be sized appropriately. Your dog should be able to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably, but there should not be so much extra room that one corner can be used as a bathroom and another as a bed. If you have a growing puppy, a crate with a divider can help you adjust the space as your puppy grows.

To use the crate effectively for housebreaking, build your day around regular potty opportunities. Puppies, especially, need frequent trips outside. Take your dog out first thing in the morning, after meals, after naps, after play sessions, and before bedtime. If your dog has been in the crate, go straight outside before doing anything else.

  • Take your dog to the same potty spot each time when possible.
  • Use a simple cue such as “Go potty.”
  • Reward immediately after your dog finishes with praise, a treat, or both.
  • Supervise closely indoors when your dog is out of the crate.
  • If you cannot supervise, use the crate for a short period until the next potty break.

Accidents can happen, especially in the early stages. If you catch your dog in the act, interrupt gently and take them outside right away. Do not yell, punish, or rub your dog’s nose in the mess. That only creates fear and confusion. Clean accidents thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner so lingering odors do not encourage repeat accidents in the same spot.

It is also important not to expect too much too soon. Very young puppies have limited bladder control. A general guideline is that a puppy may be able to hold it for about one hour per month of age during the day, though this varies by individual. Adult dogs can usually stay crated longer, but they still need regular exercise, bathroom breaks, and interaction.

Consistency is what makes crate-based housebreaking work. A predictable schedule teaches your dog where to go, when to go, and how to succeed. Over time, the crate becomes less necessary because your dog develops reliable habits.

Making Travel Easier and Safer

A crate can make travel far less stressful for your dog and much safer for everyone. Whether you are taking a short car ride, visiting family, or preparing for a longer trip, a dog who is comfortable resting in a crate is easier to transport and more likely to stay calm in unfamiliar environments.

For car travel, the crate helps prevent your dog from moving around the vehicle, distracting the driver, or being injured during sudden stops. Choose a crate that is secure, well-ventilated, and appropriate for your dog’s size. Place it on a stable surface and secure it if possible so it does not slide during turns or braking.

Before a long trip, practice with shorter rides. Let your dog enter the crate willingly, reward them, and take a brief drive around the block. Gradually increase the duration. This helps your dog learn that being crated in the car is normal and safe.

  • Do not feed a large meal right before travel if your dog gets carsick.
  • Bring water, a leash, waste bags, and a familiar blanket or toy.
  • Stop regularly on long trips for potty breaks and a chance to stretch.
  • Never leave your dog unattended in a hot car, even for a few minutes.
  • Keep the travel routine calm and matter-of-fact.

If you are staying overnight somewhere new, bringing the crate can help your dog settle more quickly. A familiar crate creates a sense of continuity in a strange place. Set it up in a quiet area, add familiar bedding, and keep your usual routines as much as possible. This can reduce barking, pacing, and nighttime restlessness.

For air travel or other more involved trips, check all requirements well in advance. Crate dimensions, labels, ventilation, and identification may all matter. Even if your dog will not travel by plane, practicing comfort in a crate prepares them for many kinds of transportation and temporary confinement.

Preparing for Vet Visits and Recovery Time

Many dogs feel stressed at the veterinary clinic because of new smells, strange sounds, handling, and unfamiliar animals. Crate training can help by giving your dog a portable safe space and making confinement less alarming when it is necessary.

If your dog already relaxes in a crate at home, bring that familiarity into the vet experience when possible. For small dogs, a crate or carrier can help them feel more secure in the waiting room and during transport. Covering part of the crate with a light blanket can reduce visual stimulation if your dog seems overwhelmed, as long as airflow remains good.

You can also prepare your dog for vet-related handling by pairing calm crate time with gentle practice at home. Briefly touch paws, ears, mouth, and body, then reward calm behavior. This does not replace veterinary care, but it can reduce anxiety about being examined.

Crate comfort becomes especially valuable after medical procedures, injuries, or illness. Your vet may recommend restricted movement during recovery. A dog who is already crate trained is much more likely to rest calmly, which can support healing and prevent re-injury.

  • Keep the recovery crate in a quiet, draft-free area.
  • Use soft bedding if your dog is allowed it and it does not interfere with treatment.
  • Offer safe chew items or enrichment approved by your vet.
  • Follow medication, feeding, and exercise instructions carefully.
  • Watch for signs of stress, pain, or discomfort and report concerns promptly.

Never wait until an emergency to introduce crate use. A dog who only experiences a crate when sick, injured, or frightened may resist it more. Regular positive crate time at home builds a foundation that pays off when life becomes unpredictable.

Teaching Everyday Calm Behavior

One of the most overlooked benefits of crate training is its role in teaching a dog how to relax. Many dogs, especially puppies and high-energy breeds, do not naturally know how to switch off. They may become overtired, overstimulated, mouthy, jumpy, or unable to settle. A crate can help create structured downtime and prevent chaos before it starts.

Think of the crate as a tool for teaching rest, not just restricting movement. After exercise, training, play, or exciting visitors, your dog may benefit from a calm break in the crate. This can prevent unwanted behaviors that often happen when a dog is tired but still mentally wound up.

To encourage calm behavior, give your dog a predictable pre-crate routine. A short potty break, a few minutes of quiet interaction, and a safe chew or food toy can help your dog settle. Keep your own energy low. If you make crate time feel dramatic, your dog may become more alert instead of more relaxed.

You can also use the crate strategically during everyday household routines. For example, if your dog gets underfoot while you cook, rushes the door when guests arrive, or becomes overexcited during children’s playtime, a short crate session can help everyone stay safe and calm. This is not about isolating your dog for convenience. It is about setting your dog up to succeed when the environment is too stimulating.

  • Use the crate before your dog becomes overly excited, not only after a problem starts.
  • Reward quiet behavior in the crate with calm praise or an occasional treat.
  • Release your dog when they are calm, not while barking or whining intensely.
  • Balance crate time with exercise, training, play, and social interaction.
  • Keep sessions appropriate for your dog’s age, energy level, and needs.

Over time, many dogs begin to seek out the crate on their own when they want rest. That is a strong sign that the crate has become a genuine comfort zone. Even if you eventually leave the crate door open most of the time, the lessons your dog learned there can carry into the rest of the home: patience, self-control, and the ability to settle.

Used wisely, the crate supports much more than basic training. It helps build routines, reduces stress in unfamiliar situations, and teaches your dog an essential life skill: how to feel safe and calm, no matter what is happening around them.

Chapter 7: Graduating from the Crate: When to Give More Freedom and How to Prevent Regression

Crate training is not meant to last forever in the same form. For most dogs, the crate begins as a management tool, a safe resting place, and a way to teach calm behavior, house training, and independence. As your dog matures and proves trustworthy, the next step is learning how to give freedom gradually without creating setbacks. This stage matters just as much as the early training process. Too much freedom too soon can lead to chewing, accidents, barking, anxiety, or a sudden dislike of being confined again.

Graduating from the crate does not mean abandoning it. In many homes, the crate remains a useful option for travel, visitors, recovery after illness, or times when a dog needs rest and structure. The goal is to help your dog earn increasing freedom while keeping the crate associated with comfort and safety. When done thoughtfully, this transition creates a dog who can relax both inside and outside the crate.

How to Know Your Dog Is Ready for More Freedom

Not every dog reaches this stage at the same age. A calm adult dog may be ready much sooner than an energetic adolescent. Readiness should be based on behavior, not wishful thinking. Before increasing freedom, your dog should show a consistent pattern of success.

Signs your dog may be ready include staying calm in the crate, having no recent potty accidents, not attempting to escape, and showing good household manners when supervised. Your dog should also be able to settle independently for short periods rather than needing constant entertainment or attention.

  • Has been reliably house trained for several weeks or longer
  • Can stay in the crate without panic, whining, or destructive behavior
  • Does not chew furniture, walls, shoes, cords, or household items when supervised
  • Can rest quietly after exercise instead of pacing constantly
  • Responds well to basic cues such as sit, down, come, and leave it
  • Shows emotional stability when left alone for short periods

If your dog still struggles with accidents, chewing, or separation-related distress, full freedom is likely premature. That does not mean training has failed. It simply means your dog still needs structure while those skills continue to develop.

A Step-by-Step Plan for Expanding Freedom

The biggest mistake many owners make is going from full crating to full house access overnight. Dogs do best with a gradual transition. Think of freedom as something your dog earns in small stages. Each step should be practiced long enough to become routine before moving to the next one.

Start by giving access to one safe, dog-proofed area outside the crate. This might be a kitchen, laundry room, or small living room section blocked off with a baby gate or exercise pen. Keep tempting items out of reach and make the space easy to supervise. Offer a bed, water if appropriate, and a safe chew or food toy.

Use this progression:

  • Step 1: Allow freedom while you are home and actively observing.
  • Step 2: Allow freedom while you are home but not constantly watching, such as while doing chores in another room for a few minutes.
  • Step 3: Leave your dog in the safe area for very short absences, such as checking the mail or taking out the trash.
  • Step 4: Gradually increase the length of those absences if your dog remains calm and appropriate.
  • Step 5: Expand access to another room only after several days or weeks of success in the first area.

Move slowly. If your dog is successful for three days, that is encouraging, but not proof they are ready for complete freedom. Aim for repeated success under slightly different conditions. Test after exercise, during quiet times, and during normal household activity. A dog who behaves well only when tired may still need more practice.

It helps to keep a simple log. Note how long your dog was free, what area they had access to, and whether there were any issues. This makes patterns easier to spot and prevents you from increasing freedom based on guesswork.

How to Prevent Regression During the Transition

Regression usually happens when owners move too fast or remove structure too completely. Dogs thrive on clarity. Even when your dog is doing well, continue using routines that support success. Keep the crate available and make it part of normal life rather than a place that appears only when something goes wrong.

One effective strategy is to continue scheduling crate time even after your dog has earned more freedom. For example, your dog might spend the night in the crate but relax outside it during the day, or have free time when you are home but still use the crate during errands. This keeps crate skills fresh and prevents your dog from seeing confinement as unusual or stressful.

To reduce the chance of setbacks:

  • Do not give access to tempting items such as laundry, trash, children’s toys, or food on counters
  • Make sure your dog gets enough physical exercise and mental enrichment every day
  • Use food puzzles, chew items, and calm routines before alone time
  • Practice short departures regularly instead of only leaving for long absences
  • Reward calm behavior in the house, not just obedience cues
  • Keep greetings and departures low-key to avoid building excitement or anxiety

Watch for early warning signs of regression. These can include sniffing for potty spots indoors, renewed chewing, pacing, barking after you leave, or inability to settle. Addressing these signs early is much easier than fixing a fully developed problem. If you notice them, reduce freedom temporarily and return to the last successful stage.

Remember that life changes can affect your dog’s behavior. Moving homes, schedule shifts, guests, travel, adolescence, illness, and reduced exercise can all lead to setbacks. During these times, returning to more crate use is not a punishment. It is smart management while your dog regains stability.

What to Do If Problems Return

Setbacks are common, especially in adolescent dogs. The right response is calm, practical, and immediate. If your dog has an accident, destroys something, or shows distress when left loose, do not assume they are being stubborn or spiteful. More often, the dog was given a level of freedom they were not yet ready to handle.

Go back one or two steps in the process. If your dog had trouble alone in the living room, return to a smaller safe area. If they struggled loose in a gated room, use the crate again for absences while you rebuild skills. Then practice success in shorter sessions.

Ask yourself a few honest questions:

  • Was the area truly dog-proofed?
  • Did my dog get enough exercise before being left alone?
  • Did I increase time or space too quickly?
  • Is my dog showing signs of separation anxiety rather than simple lack of training?
  • Has anything changed in our routine recently?

If the issue is mild, a slower plan is often enough. If your dog is panicking, drooling excessively, trying to escape, injuring themselves, or showing severe distress, this may be more than a crate transition problem. In that case, consult a qualified trainer or veterinary behavior professional. Anxiety-based behavior requires a different approach than ordinary household training.

Even after your dog has largely graduated from the crate, keep practicing positive crate experiences. Toss treats in occasionally, feed meals there from time to time, or let your dog nap with the door open. This preserves the crate as a familiar, safe place rather than something your dog “outgrew” and now resists.

Graduation is not a single moment. It is a process of building trust, testing readiness, and protecting the habits you worked hard to create. By increasing freedom gradually and responding quickly to small problems, you can help your dog become reliable in the home without losing the benefits of crate training. The best outcome is not simply a dog who no longer needs a crate, but a dog who feels secure, behaves responsibly, and can adapt to both structure and freedom.

Key Takeaways

  • Increase freedom based on consistent behavior, not age alone.
  • Expand access gradually, starting with one safe, dog-proofed area.
  • Keep using the crate in a positive way even as your dog earns more freedom.
  • Prevent regression by maintaining routines, exercise, enrichment, and low-key departures.
  • If problems return, reduce freedom immediately and go back to the last successful step.
  • Use the crate as a management and comfort tool, not as punishment.
