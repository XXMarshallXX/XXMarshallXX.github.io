Why Crate Training Works and How It Helps Your Dog

Crate training is one of the most useful and misunderstood tools in dog training. When introduced properly, a crate is not a punishment or a place of isolation. It becomes a safe, predictable space where your dog can relax, settle, and feel secure. Dogs naturally seek out small, sheltered resting areas, and a crate can meet that instinctive need while also helping you teach good habits at home.

For new puppy owners, crate training can make daily life much easier. For adult dogs, it can provide structure, improve safety, and reduce stress during travel, recovery, or changes in routine. A crate helps your dog learn how to be calm, how to hold their bladder for appropriate periods, and how to rest without constantly following every movement in the home. It also gives you peace of mind when you cannot supervise directly.

The key is to understand why crate training works and how to use it in a way that supports your dog emotionally and physically. A crate should always be associated with comfort, rest, and rewards. When used thoughtfully, it becomes a foundation for house training, independence, and better behavior throughout the day.

The Crate Appeals to Your Dog’s Natural Instincts

Dogs are den-oriented animals in the sense that they often prefer enclosed, quiet places for rest. While your dog does not need a den in the wild, many dogs feel more secure when they have a defined personal space. A crate can recreate that feeling indoors by offering a calm area away from noise, foot traffic, and overstimulation.

In a busy household, dogs are exposed to many triggers: people coming and going, children playing, doorbells ringing, and constant activity. Without a place to retreat, some dogs become overtired, anxious, or reactive. A crate creates a simple message: this is where you can switch off and rest. Over time, many dogs will choose to enter the crate on their own because they associate it with calmness and safety.

To make this instinct work in your favor, set up the crate as a comfortable retreat. Practical steps include:

  • Place the crate in a quiet but not isolated part of the home, such as a living room corner or bedroom.
  • Add soft bedding if your dog does not chew or shred fabric.
  • Include a safe chew toy or food puzzle to build positive associations.
  • Keep the environment around the crate calm, especially during the first few days.
  • Allow your dog to explore the crate freely before closing the door for longer periods.

A crate should never feel like a trap. Let your dog enter at their own pace, use treats generously, and praise calm behavior. The goal is for the crate to become a place your dog wants to be, not a place they fear.

Crate Training Supports House Training and Routine

One of the biggest reasons crate training works so well is that it helps with house training. Most dogs naturally avoid soiling the area where they sleep. A properly sized crate encourages your dog to hold their bladder until they are taken outside, which helps build reliable bathroom habits.

The crate should be large enough for your dog to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably, but not so large that one end becomes a sleeping area and the other becomes a toilet corner. This is especially important for puppies, who are still developing physical control and need frequent bathroom breaks.

Crate training works best when paired with a consistent schedule. Instead of waiting for accidents, you proactively guide your dog toward success. Take your dog outside:

  • First thing in the morning
  • After naps
  • After meals
  • After play sessions
  • Before bedtime
  • Immediately after being let out of the crate

When your dog eliminates outside, reward them right away with praise, treats, or both. This creates a clear connection between going to the bathroom in the right place and receiving something positive. If accidents happen indoors, clean them thoroughly and avoid punishment. Punishment can create fear and confusion, while consistency builds understanding.

The crate also helps establish a healthy daily rhythm. Dogs thrive on routine. Scheduled crate time for naps, downtime, or quiet chewing teaches your dog that not every moment is for play or attention. This can be especially valuable for puppies, who often become mouthy and wild when overtired. Many behavior issues improve simply because the dog is finally getting enough rest.

A Crate Builds Safety, Independence, and Better Behavior

Crates are not just useful for puppies. They are practical safety tools for dogs of all ages. When you cannot supervise your dog, a crate prevents access to hazards such as electrical cords, trash, toxic foods, shoes, medications, and household cleaners. This protection is especially important during the early months, when curiosity is high and judgment is low.

Crates also help prevent your dog from rehearsing unwanted behaviors. Every time a dog chews furniture, steals socks, raids the bin, or barks at the window for long periods, that behavior can become more established. A crate interrupts those habits and gives you a way to manage the environment while your dog is still learning.

Just as importantly, crate training can teach emotional independence. Many dogs struggle when they are never asked to settle alone. If your dog is constantly with you, they may become distressed the moment you leave the room or the house. Short, positive crate sessions help your dog learn that being alone is normal and safe.

To build independence successfully:

  • Start with very short crate sessions while you remain nearby.
  • Give your dog a stuffed food toy or chew before closing the door.
  • Leave the room briefly, then return before your dog becomes upset.
  • Gradually increase the time apart in small steps.
  • Return calmly so your arrivals and departures feel routine, not dramatic.

This gradual approach is far more effective than placing a dog in a crate for long periods too soon. The crate should teach calmness, not panic. With patience, your dog learns that quiet time alone is part of normal life and that you always come back.

How to Use the Crate Humanely and Successfully

The success of crate training depends on how you introduce and use the crate. A crate is helpful only when it is part of a balanced routine that includes exercise, mental stimulation, social interaction, and training. Dogs should not spend excessive hours confined with no outlet for energy or companionship.

Begin by making the crate rewarding. Toss treats inside, feed meals near or in the crate, and let your dog investigate without pressure. Once your dog is comfortable entering, begin closing the door for a few seconds while they enjoy a treat or chew. Slowly increase duration over several sessions.

Watch your dog’s body language. Relaxed posture, soft eyes, lying down, and quiet chewing are good signs. Whining, frantic scratching, heavy panting, or attempts to escape may mean you are progressing too quickly. If that happens, shorten the sessions and rebuild confidence more gradually.

Here are some practical rules for humane crate training:

  • Never use the crate as punishment after your dog has done something wrong.
  • Always give your dog a chance to toilet before crate time.
  • Make sure your dog has had enough exercise and mental activity for their age and breed.
  • Use the crate for short, positive sessions at first.
  • Provide water when appropriate and safe, especially for longer periods.
  • Keep the crate clean, well-ventilated, and comfortable.
  • Balance crate time with plenty of freedom, training, and companionship outside the crate.

It is also important to be realistic. Crate training is not a shortcut that replaces training or attention. It is a management and teaching tool. Used correctly, it helps your dog learn self-control, rest quietly, and feel secure. Used carelessly, it can create frustration. Your dog’s age, temperament, history, and energy level all matter, so adjust your pace to the individual dog in front of you.

When approached with patience and consistency, crate training offers benefits that reach far beyond the crate itself. It can make vet visits, travel, grooming appointments, boarding, and recovery from illness or surgery much less stressful. It can support better sleep, fewer accidents, and a calmer home overall. Most of all, it gives your dog a dependable place to relax and feel safe in your care.

Key Takeaways

  • A crate works best when your dog sees it as a safe, comfortable resting space.
  • Crate training supports house training by encouraging bladder control and routine.
  • It helps prevent unsafe behavior and protects your dog when you cannot supervise.
  • Short, positive crate sessions can build independence and reduce stress when alone.
  • Never use the crate as punishment; introduce it gradually with treats, calmness, and consistency.
  • Successful crate training always includes exercise, mental stimulation, and plenty of time outside the crate.

Chapter 2: Choosing the Right Crate, Setup, and Essential Supplies

Before crate training can be successful, you need the right crate, the right placement, and a few smart supplies that help your dog feel safe and comfortable. Many crate training problems begin long before the dog ever steps inside. A crate that is too large, too small, poorly placed, or filled with the wrong items can make the process harder than it needs to be. The good news is that setting things up correctly from the start is simple once you know what to look for.

In this chapter, you will learn how to choose a crate that fits your dog’s size, age, and temperament, where to place it in your home, and which supplies make crate training easier. Think of the crate as your dog’s bedroom, not a storage box or a punishment tool. When chosen and arranged thoughtfully, it becomes a calm, secure place your dog can relax, sleep, and settle.

Pick the Right Type and Size of Crate

There is no single perfect crate for every dog. The best choice depends on your dog’s age, chewing habits, travel needs, and personality. Some dogs like the open visibility of a wire crate, while others settle better in a more enclosed plastic kennel. Soft-sided crates can work well for calm dogs, but they are usually not ideal for puppies or heavy chewers.

Here are the most common crate types:

  • Wire crates: Excellent for home use, good airflow, easy visibility, often include divider panels for growing puppies.
  • Plastic crates: More enclosed and den-like, often preferred for travel, useful for dogs who relax better with fewer visual distractions.
  • Soft-sided crates: Lightweight and portable, best for crate-trained dogs who are calm and unlikely to scratch or chew their way out.
  • Furniture-style crates: Designed to blend into home decor, but should still be sturdy, well-ventilated, and properly sized.

Size matters more than many owners realize. A crate should be large enough for your dog to stand up without crouching, turn around comfortably, and lie down fully stretched out. It should not be so large that your dog can sleep at one end and use the other end as a bathroom. This is especially important with puppies, who may need a smaller usable space while learning bladder control.

If you are crate training a puppy, choose a crate that will fit the dog’s adult size and use a divider panel to reduce the available space at first. As your puppy grows and becomes more reliable, you can gradually expand the interior. This saves money and helps maintain consistent crate habits.

When shopping, measure your dog from nose to base of tail and from floor to top of head or ears while standing. Add a few inches to each measurement for comfort. If your dog is between sizes, it is often better to choose the next size up, then adjust with a divider if necessary.

Set Up the Crate for Comfort and Success

Once you have the crate, your next job is to make it inviting. The goal is to create a space that feels calm, comfortable, and safe. A well-prepared crate encourages your dog to enter willingly and remain relaxed inside.

Start with the flooring. Some dogs are comfortable on the crate tray alone, but many do better with a washable crate pad, mat, or folded blanket. For puppies or dogs that chew bedding, keep it simple at first. Safety always comes before comfort. If your dog shreds fabric or tries to eat stuffing, remove soft bedding and use a durable, chew-resistant mat or no bedding until the habit improves.

You can also make the crate feel more secure by partially covering it with a light blanket or crate cover, especially if your dog is easily overstimulated. Be sure not to block ventilation. Some dogs settle faster with reduced visual input, while others may become nervous if the crate feels too enclosed. Watch your dog’s response and adjust accordingly.

Helpful setup tips include:

  • Keep the crate clean and dry at all times.
  • Use bedding that is easy to wash.
  • Secure water bowls only if needed and only when appropriate for your dog’s age and schedule.
  • Remove collars, tags, or harnesses before crating if they could catch on crate bars.
  • Add a safe chew or food-stuffed toy during training sessions to build positive associations.

A crate should never feel chaotic or unpredictable. Avoid tossing your dog in with loud scolding or using the crate only when you leave the house. Instead, build pleasant experiences in and around it. Feed meals near the crate, then inside the crate. Drop treats in randomly. Praise calm behavior. The more your dog associates the crate with comfort and rewards, the faster training progresses.

Choose the Best Location in Your Home

Where you put the crate has a major effect on how your dog feels about it. Dogs are social animals, and most do not want to be isolated in a distant room for long periods. A good crate location balances companionship with quiet.

For most homes, the ideal place is a low-traffic area of a family room, living room, or kitchen where your dog can see and hear household activity without being in the middle of constant commotion. During the day, this allows your dog to feel included while still having a place to rest. At night, many owners place a crate in the bedroom or nearby hallway, especially for puppies. This can reduce nighttime stress, help with potty training, and make it easier to respond before whining escalates.

Avoid placing the crate in direct sunlight, next to heating vents, in a drafty area, or near loud televisions, speakers, or slamming doors. Temperature and noise matter. Dogs rest best in a location that feels stable and predictable.

If your home has more than one level, you may eventually want more than one crate or a crate plus a gated resting area, especially during the early stages of training. This can make supervision easier and help your dog settle near you throughout the day.

When testing a location, ask yourself:

  • Is the area quiet enough for rest?
  • Can my dog still feel part of the household?
  • Is the crate protected from excess heat, cold, and drafts?
  • Can I easily supervise and access my dog?
  • Does this location support daytime naps and nighttime sleep?

Do not be afraid to adjust. If your dog seems unable to settle, the issue may not be the crate itself but where it is placed.

Gather the Essential Supplies

You do not need to buy every dog product on the market, but a few well-chosen supplies can make crate training smoother and more enjoyable. Focus on items that support safety, comfort, cleanliness, and positive reinforcement.

Essential crate training supplies include:

  • Crate divider: Useful for puppies growing into a larger crate.
  • Washable bedding or crate mat: Choose something durable and easy to clean.
  • Safe chew toys: Help relieve boredom and create calm crate time.
  • Food-stuffable toys: Excellent for building positive associations and encouraging relaxation.
  • Treat pouch or small training treats: Makes rewarding crate entry quick and consistent.
  • Enzyme cleaner: Essential for cleaning accidents thoroughly and removing odor.
  • Light crate cover: Optional, but helpful for some dogs.
  • Portable water solution: Especially useful for travel or longer supervised crate periods.

Choose toys carefully. Anything placed in the crate should be safe for unsupervised use based on your dog’s chewing style. Avoid toys with loose parts, strings, or anything your dog can break into sharp pieces. If you are unsure, supervise first before leaving the item in the crate during quiet time.

It is also wise to keep a simple crate routine kit nearby. This might include treats, poop bags, cleaning spray, spare bedding, and one or two approved chew items. Having everything in one place makes training easier and helps you stay consistent.

Finally, remember that supplies support training, but they do not replace it. A comfortable crate and a stuffed toy are helpful, but your dog still needs gradual introduction, patience, and repetition. The setup creates the opportunity; your daily habits create the results.

By choosing the correct crate, placing it thoughtfully, and stocking a few useful supplies, you give your dog the best possible start. In the next chapter, we will move into the first training steps that teach your dog to see the crate as a positive, rewarding place to be.

Key Takeaways

  • Choose a crate that allows your dog to stand, turn, and lie down comfortably without giving too much extra space.
  • Use a divider for puppies so the crate grows with them while still supporting house training.
  • Set up the crate with safe, washable bedding and avoid items your dog may chew or swallow.
  • Place the crate in a calm area where your dog can feel included but not overwhelmed.
  • Use practical supplies like chew toys, food-stuffable toys, treats, and enzyme cleaner to support training.
  • Make the crate feel like a safe bedroom, not a punishment space.

Chapter 3: The First 7 Days: Introducing the Crate Without Stress

The first week of crate training sets the tone for everything that follows. If your dog learns that the crate is calm, safe, and predictable, training becomes much easier. If the crate feels forced, frightening, or isolating, progress can slow down quickly. The goal during these first seven days is not to make your dog “love” the crate instantly or stay inside for long periods. The goal is to build trust, create positive associations, and introduce short, successful experiences.

Think of the crate as a skill your dog needs to learn, not a place they should automatically understand. Puppies, rescue dogs, and even confident adult dogs may need time to adjust. Move at your dog’s pace. A relaxed, gradual approach almost always works better than trying to rush the process.

Set Up the Crate for Success Before Day One

Before you begin training, make the crate inviting and practical. Placement matters. Put the crate in a part of the home where your dog can see and hear the family, such as the living room or bedroom. A crate placed in a remote, quiet corner may feel more like isolation than comfort, especially in the beginning.

Add soft bedding if your dog is likely to leave it alone. If your dog chews bedding, keep the setup simple and safe. You can also place a few treats inside, along with a favorite toy or chew. Leave the door open at first so your dog can investigate without pressure.

Here are a few helpful setup tips:

  • Choose a crate large enough for your dog to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably.
  • Keep the crate clean, dry, and free of anything unsafe to chew or swallow.
  • Use high-value treats your dog does not get all the time.
  • Cover part of the crate with a light blanket if your dog relaxes better in a den-like space, but ensure good airflow.
  • Keep the atmosphere calm when introducing the crate. Avoid crowding, loud excitement, or forcing your dog inside.

If your dog shows caution, that is normal. Curiosity often comes before confidence. Let your dog sniff, look, and step in and out as they choose. The crate should feel like their decision, not your demand.

A Simple Day-by-Day Plan for the First Week

You do not need marathon training sessions. Short, frequent sessions work best. Aim for several mini-practice periods each day, each lasting just a few minutes. End while your dog is still comfortable and successful.

Days 1 and 2: Exploration and rewards

Leave the crate door open and toss treats just inside the entrance. If your dog steps in to get them, praise gently. Feed a few treats in a row while they are inside, then let them come back out. You can also feed meals near the crate or just inside it. At this stage, avoid closing the door. Focus only on helping your dog see the crate as a good place.

Days 3 and 4: Short stays with the door open

Once your dog willingly enters the crate, begin encouraging slightly longer stays. Give a stuffed food toy, chew, or a few treats one at a time while they remain inside. Sit nearby so your dog feels secure. If they choose to lie down, that is an excellent sign. Continue leaving the door open.

Days 5 and 6: Brief door closure

When your dog is relaxed inside, gently close the door for just a few seconds while giving treats through the bars or immediately reopening it before your dog becomes worried. Gradually increase to 10, 20, or 30 seconds if your dog stays calm. The key is to open the door before whining, pawing, or panic begins.

Day 7: Very short separation practice

With your dog in the crate enjoying a chew or treat, close the door and step one or two steps away. Return almost immediately and reward calm behavior. Over several repetitions, increase the distance or briefly step out of sight for a second or two. Keep sessions short and easy. You are teaching your dog that you always come back and that calm behavior makes good things happen.

If your dog struggles at any point, go back one step. Progress is not always perfectly linear. A dog who was comfortable yesterday may need a little more support today, and that is completely normal.

How to Read Your Dog’s Stress Signals

Successful crate training depends on paying attention to body language. Mild hesitation is normal, but clear stress signals mean you should slow down. Your dog does not need to be perfectly happy every second, but they should be able to recover quickly and stay mostly relaxed.

Watch for these signs of stress:

  • Refusing treats they would normally eat
  • Backing away from the crate or freezing near it
  • Heavy panting when the room is cool
  • Whining that escalates instead of settling
  • Pawing frantically at the door
  • Drooling, trembling, or repeated attempts to escape
  • Sudden barking or agitation when the door closes

If you notice these signs, do not punish your dog or force them to “push through it.” Instead, make the exercise easier. Open the door sooner, sit closer, shorten the session, or return to feeding treats with the door open. Crate training should build confidence, not endurance.

It also helps to train when your dog is already somewhat calm. After light exercise, a potty break, and a few minutes of quiet time is often ideal. Trying crate practice when your dog is overexcited, overtired, or full of energy can make training harder than it needs to be.

Common Mistakes to Avoid During the First Week

Many crate training problems begin with good intentions but poor timing. Avoiding a few common mistakes can save you and your dog a lot of frustration.

  • Do not force your dog into the crate. Luring with treats is fine. Pushing, dragging, or trapping your dog inside creates fear.
  • Do not use the crate as punishment. The crate should never become the place your dog is sent when you are angry.
  • Do not leave your dog crated too long too soon. The first week is for short sessions, not extended confinement.
  • Do not move too quickly. Closing the door for several minutes before your dog is ready can create setbacks.
  • Do not reward frantic behavior by changing the plan in a dramatic way. Instead, work below your dog’s stress threshold so distress does not build.

One practical strategy is to build crate time into your daily routine in natural ways. Offer a chew in the crate while you sit nearby reading or working. Feed part of your dog’s meals in the crate. Let naps happen there when your dog is tired. These quiet, ordinary moments teach your dog that the crate is part of normal life.

If nighttime crate training is part of your plan, place the crate near your bed at first. Many dogs settle better when they can hear and smell their people. A young puppy may need one or more nighttime potty breaks, so keeping the crate close helps you respond before your puppy becomes fully distressed.

Remember that crate training is not about making your dog stay alone for long hours. It is about creating a safe resting space and a management tool that your dog can tolerate comfortably. The first week should feel gentle, predictable, and encouraging.

By the end of these seven days, your dog may not be ready for long crate periods, and that is okay. What matters most is that your dog is beginning to enter willingly, remain calm for short periods, and trust that the crate is a safe place. That foundation will make the next stages of training much smoother.

Key Takeaways

  • Focus on positive associations during the first week, not long confinement.
  • Keep the crate comfortable, accessible, and located near family activity.
  • Use short, frequent sessions with treats, meals, toys, and calm praise.
  • Close the crate door only after your dog is relaxed entering and staying inside.
  • Watch for stress signals and make training easier if your dog seems overwhelmed.
  • Never force the crate or use it as punishment.
  • Progress at your dog’s pace and build trust first; duration can come later.

Chapter 4: Building Positive Crate Habits with Routines, Rewards, and Timing

A crate becomes truly useful when your dog sees it as a normal, safe, and rewarding part of daily life. That does not happen by accident. Positive crate habits are built through repetition, predictable routines, thoughtful rewards, and careful timing. When these pieces work together, the crate stops feeling like confinement and starts feeling like a place where good things happen.

Many crate training problems begin when owners move too quickly, use the crate only when leaving the house, or accidentally reward barking and resistance. The good news is that dogs learn patterns fast. If you consistently connect the crate with calmness, comfort, and success, your dog will begin to enter it more willingly and settle more easily. In this chapter, you will learn how to create those positive habits in practical, everyday ways.

Create a Predictable Daily Crate Routine

Dogs thrive on consistency. A regular schedule helps your dog understand when it is time to play, eat, rest, go outside, and settle in the crate. When the crate is part of a predictable routine, it feels less stressful because it is no longer a surprise. Instead of wondering what is happening, your dog begins to expect the pattern.

Start by identifying the times of day when your dog naturally needs rest or quiet time. Puppies often need crate time after play sessions, meals, training, and bathroom breaks. Adult dogs may benefit from crate time during household busy periods, while guests visit, or when they need a calm place to relax. The goal is not to crate your dog constantly, but to make crate use regular enough that it feels familiar.

A simple routine might include a potty break, a few minutes of calm interaction, then a short crate rest with a chew or treat. Repeating this sequence teaches your dog that the crate follows normal daily events. This predictability reduces anxiety and helps prevent resistance.

  • Take your dog outside before crate time whenever possible.
  • Use the crate after exercise, training, or play, when your dog is more ready to rest.
  • Feed meals near or inside the crate to build a positive association.
  • Include short crate sessions even when you are home, not only when you leave.
  • Release your dog when calm, so calm behavior becomes part of the routine.

Try to avoid using the crate only when something unpleasant happens, such as when visitors arrive, when the household becomes chaotic, or right before you disappear for hours. If the crate predicts isolation every time, your dog may begin to dread it. Balanced use is important. Include crate time during ordinary, peaceful moments so the crate becomes a normal feature of daily life.

Use Rewards That Build Real Enthusiasm

Rewards are one of the fastest ways to change how your dog feels about the crate. If entering the crate leads to food, praise, toys, and rest, your dog begins to see the crate as valuable. The key is to reward generously at first, then gradually shift toward maintaining the habit rather than constantly bribing.

Begin by rewarding every step in the right direction. If your dog looks at the crate, mark that with cheerful praise. If your dog takes a step toward it, reward again. If your dog puts one paw inside, reward. Then reward for entering fully, turning around, and lying down. This step-by-step approach is especially useful for hesitant dogs.

Use rewards your dog truly cares about. For some dogs, kibble is enough. For others, you may need small pieces of chicken, cheese, or a favorite chew. Soft bedding, a stuffed food toy, or a safe long-lasting chew can also make crate time more appealing. The more your dog enjoys what happens inside the crate, the stronger the positive association becomes.

  • Toss treats into the crate so your dog chooses to go in voluntarily.
  • Feed special chew items only in the crate to increase its value.
  • Praise calmly when your dog settles, rather than exciting your dog too much.
  • Use a cue such as “crate,” “bed,” or “kennel” once your dog begins to understand the action.
  • Reward relaxed body language, not just physical entry.

One common mistake is luring the dog into the crate and then immediately shutting the door before the dog is comfortable. This can make your dog suspicious of rewards. Instead, spend time rewarding crate entry without always closing the door. Let your dog go in, eat a treat, and come back out. Then repeat. This teaches that entering the crate is safe and does not always mean being trapped.

As your dog improves, vary the rewards but do not remove them too quickly. You want the crate habit to remain strong. Over time, the crate itself becomes rewarding because it predicts rest, security, and pleasant experiences.

Master Timing: When to Crate, When to Release, and When to Ignore

Timing can make or break crate training. Dogs connect events that happen close together, so your actions in the seconds before and after crate use matter a great deal. Good timing helps your dog understand exactly what behavior earns freedom, attention, and rewards.

First, choose the right moment to introduce crate time. It is usually easier to crate a dog after physical exercise, mental stimulation, or a bathroom break. A dog with excess energy is far less likely to settle. If your puppy has been running around wildly for an hour and has not gone outside, crate time may trigger frustration, barking, or accidents. Set your dog up to succeed by meeting basic needs first.

Second, pay attention to how and when you close the crate door. If your dog enters willingly, give a reward, pause for a second, and close the door calmly. Stay nearby at first. Then reward a few moments of quiet. You are teaching that calm behavior makes good things continue.

Third, be strategic about release timing. If you open the crate door while your dog is barking, scratching, or throwing a tantrum, your dog may learn that noise opens the door. Instead, wait for a brief pause, even one or two seconds of quiet, then release. This does not mean letting a distressed dog panic for long periods. It means looking for a small moment of calm and reinforcing that.

  • Crate after a potty break, not before one.
  • Reward calmness within the crate, especially in the first few minutes.
  • Release during quiet moments whenever possible.
  • Keep departures and arrivals low-key to avoid increasing excitement.
  • Increase crate duration gradually rather than in big jumps.

If your dog whines briefly when crated, pause before responding. Some dogs make a small protest as they settle. If you immediately rush over and open the door, you can accidentally teach your dog to vocalize for attention. On the other hand, if your dog is escalating into real panic, the session may be too difficult. In that case, shorten the duration next time and rebuild more slowly.

Good timing also applies to your own emotions. Stay calm and matter-of-fact. If you act worried, apologetic, or dramatic every time you crate your dog, your dog may sense that something is wrong. Treat crate time as a normal, positive part of the day.

Troubleshoot Habits Before They Become Problems

Positive crate habits are easier to maintain than to repair. That is why it is important to notice early warning signs and adjust quickly. If your dog starts hesitating at the crate door, refusing treats inside, or vocalizing more than usual, do not assume the dog is being stubborn. Ask what may have changed.

Sometimes the issue is physical discomfort. The crate may be too small, too warm, too cold, or placed in a noisy area. Your dog may need more exercise, more bathroom breaks, or shorter sessions. Puppies in particular can outgrow a routine quickly and need frequent adjustments.

Sometimes the problem is emotional. If every crate session suddenly leads to long absences, your dog may begin to associate the crate with loneliness. Reintroduce easy wins by doing short, positive crate sessions while you remain nearby. Give your dog chances to succeed again.

Keep a simple training log if needed. Write down when your dog was crated, for how long, what happened before the session, and how your dog responded. Patterns often become clear when you track them. You may discover that your dog settles well in the morning but struggles in the evening, or that crate sessions go better after sniff walks than after rough play.

  • If your dog resists entering, go back to rewarding small steps toward the crate.
  • If barking increases, reduce crate duration and rebuild gradually.
  • If accidents happen, adjust potty timing and avoid keeping your dog crated too long.
  • If your dog seems restless, add more physical and mental enrichment before crate time.
  • If your dog only tolerates the crate when exhausted, spend more time building positive associations.

Most setbacks are not failures. They are feedback. When you respond by simplifying the process and making the crate easier and more rewarding again, your dog can regain confidence quickly. Patience and consistency are more effective than forcing progress.

Remember that the long-term goal is not just compliance. It is comfort. A dog that runs into the crate for a treat but remains tense has not fully learned to relax. A dog with positive crate habits can enter willingly, settle calmly, and rest without fear. That kind of trust is built one routine, one reward, and one well-timed success at a time.

  • Reward crate entry, calm behavior, and relaxed settling with treats, praise, and safe chew items.
  • Choose good timing by crating after exercise and potty breaks, and releasing during quiet moments.

  • Include short, positive crate sessions when you are home so the crate does not only predict your absence.

Chapter 5: Using the Crate for House Training, Sleep, and Daily Management

A crate is one of the most practical tools you can use in everyday dog training. When introduced correctly, it helps your dog learn where to rest, when to settle, and where not to eliminate. It also gives you a safe, predictable way to manage your dog during busy parts of the day. In this chapter, you will learn how to use the crate for house training, nighttime sleep, and daily routines without creating stress or overdependence.

The key to success is to think of the crate as a support system, not a punishment. Dogs naturally avoid soiling the place where they sleep, which makes the crate especially useful during house training. At the same time, the crate can help prevent chewing, scavenging, or overstimulation when you cannot supervise directly. Used thoughtfully, it teaches good habits and gives your dog a clear structure.

Using the Crate for House Training

House training works best when your dog has a consistent schedule and limited opportunities to make mistakes. A properly sized crate supports this process because it encourages your dog to hold their bladder and bowels for a reasonable period. The crate should be large enough for your dog to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably, but not so large that one end becomes a sleeping area and the other a bathroom.

When using the crate for house training, timing matters more than anything else. Puppies, especially, need frequent bathroom breaks. Adult dogs may also need a structured routine if they are newly adopted or adjusting to a new home. Every trip out of the crate should be purposeful. Instead of opening the door and letting your dog wander around the house, take them straight to the designated potty area.

Follow a simple pattern: crate, potty break, supervised freedom, then back to the crate if you cannot supervise. This prevents accidents and teaches your dog that outdoor elimination is the goal. Praise warmly and reward immediately after they finish in the correct spot. The closer the reward is to the behavior, the faster your dog will understand what earns success.

  • Take your dog out first thing in the morning.
  • Take them out immediately after naps.
  • Take them out after meals and big drinks of water.
  • Take them out after play sessions or excitement.
  • Take them out right before bedtime.
  • Take them out whenever they come out of the crate.

If your dog has an accident, do not scold after the fact. Clean the area thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner and adjust your routine. Accidents usually mean your dog had too much freedom, too much time between potty breaks, or not enough supervision. The crate helps you tighten the process so your dog can succeed more often.

Watch for signs that your dog may need to go out, such as circling, sniffing, wandering away, whining, or suddenly stopping play. If you are unsure, take them outside. It is always better to offer an extra potty trip than to risk an accident indoors.

Making the Crate Part of a Calm Sleep Routine

The crate can be an excellent sleep space because it creates a quiet, secure environment. Many dogs sleep more soundly in a crate because there is less stimulation and fewer opportunities to roam, bark at noises, or get into trouble. For puppies, the crate is especially useful at night because it helps establish a consistent bedtime routine and reduces overnight accidents.

Set up the crate in a comfortable location. For a new puppy or newly adopted dog, placing the crate in or near your bedroom can be very helpful. Your presence often reduces anxiety and makes nighttime settling easier. As your dog becomes more confident, you can decide whether the crate remains there or moves to another area of the home.

Before bedtime, give your dog a chance to burn off a little energy, then provide a final potty break. Keep the evening calm. Avoid intense play right before bed, as this can make settling harder. A short walk, a quiet chew, or a few minutes of gentle petting can help your dog transition into sleep mode.

  • Feed dinner early enough to allow a final bathroom trip before bed.
  • Offer water as normal during the day, but be mindful of large late-night drinks if your puppy is struggling overnight.
  • Use a consistent bedtime each night.
  • Add soft bedding if your dog does not chew or shred it.
  • Consider a crate cover if it helps your dog relax, but ensure good ventilation.

If your puppy cries at night, pause before responding. Some puppies fuss briefly and then settle. If the crying continues, your puppy may need a bathroom break. Take them out quietly, without play or excitement, then return them to the crate. Keep nighttime potty trips boring so your dog learns that nighttime is for sleeping, not socializing.

Avoid bringing your dog out of the crate every time they make a small noise. If you do, you may accidentally teach them that fussing opens the door. Your goal is to be responsive to genuine needs while still encouraging self-settling. With consistency, most dogs learn the nighttime routine quickly.

Using the Crate for Daily Management

Crate training is not only about house training and sleep. It is also a powerful daily management tool. Real life includes work calls, errands, meal preparation, visitors, and moments when full supervision is impossible. The crate gives your dog a safe place to be during those times. It prevents rehearsal of unwanted behaviors like chewing furniture, stealing laundry, pestering children, or racing around the house overstimulated.

Daily management works best when the crate is part of a balanced routine. Your dog should still get exercise, training, enrichment, and social interaction. The crate is there to support those activities, not replace them. A dog who has had a walk, a sniffing opportunity, a short training session, and a chew toy is much more likely to rest calmly in the crate than a dog who has been bored all day.

You can use the crate strategically during predictable moments:

  • During meals, if your dog begs or counter-surfs.
  • When guests arrive and your dog becomes overexcited.
  • During cleaning, repairs, or deliveries.
  • When young children are active and you need a management break.
  • After exercise, to encourage rest and prevent overtired behavior.
  • When you leave the house for short periods, if your dog is comfortable being crated.

To make daily crating easier, prepare the environment before your dog goes in. Offer a stuffed food toy, a safe chew, or a small scatter of treats in the crate. This creates a positive association and helps your dog settle. Keep your departures and returns low-key. Dramatic goodbyes can increase anxiety, while calm routines teach your dog that crate time is normal and temporary.

It is also useful to build short crate sessions into the day even when you are home. This teaches your dog that being in the crate does not always mean you are leaving. Sit nearby, read, work, or move around the room while your dog relaxes in the crate with something enjoyable. This reduces the chance that the crate becomes linked only to isolation.

Avoiding Common Mistakes and Building Good Habits

The crate is most effective when used with fairness and consistency. One common mistake is leaving a dog crated too long. Dogs need movement, mental stimulation, bathroom breaks, and human interaction. Long periods of confinement can lead to frustration, stress, or setbacks in training. Another mistake is using the crate only when the dog has done something wrong. That can poison the association and make the crate feel like a place of punishment.

Instead, aim to create a rhythm your dog can predict. Alternate activity with rest. Give clear potty opportunities. Reward calm behavior. Respect your dog’s age, bladder capacity, and emotional comfort level. If your dog suddenly begins resisting the crate, barking more than usual, or having accidents despite previous progress, step back and look for causes such as illness, stress, poor timing, or too much crate time.

Helpful habits to maintain include:

  • Keep a regular potty and feeding schedule.
  • Make crate entries positive with treats, toys, or meals.
  • Use the crate before your dog becomes wild or overtired.
  • Release your dog when they are calm whenever possible.
  • Gradually increase crate duration rather than jumping too fast.
  • Pair crate time with exercise and enrichment outside the crate.

Remember that the long-term goal is not simply to keep your dog in a crate. The goal is to teach self-control, clean habits, calm sleep, and safe downtime. As your dog matures and proves trustworthy, you may rely on the crate less. Even then, many dogs continue to enjoy it as a familiar resting place.

When used thoughtfully, the crate becomes much more than a training box. It becomes a foundation for a well-managed home, a smoother house training process, and a dog who knows how to relax. That is why crate training, done kindly and consistently, is such a valuable skill for both dogs and owners.

Chapter 6: Solving Common Problems: Whining, Barking, Anxiety, and Resistance

Even when crate training starts well, most dog owners run into a few bumps along the way. A puppy may cry the moment the door closes. An older dog may bark in protest. Some dogs seem nervous and restless, while others refuse to enter the crate at all. These problems are common, and in most cases they can be improved with patience, consistency, and a better understanding of what your dog is communicating.

The most important thing to remember is that problem behavior around the crate usually has a cause. Your dog is not trying to be difficult or manipulative. Whining, barking, and resistance often mean your dog is confused, overstimulated, under-exercised, anxious, or simply not yet comfortable with the crate. Instead of forcing the issue, focus on making the crate feel safe, predictable, and rewarding.

In this chapter, you will learn how to respond to common crate training challenges without making them worse. The goal is not just to get your dog inside the crate, but to help your dog relax there willingly.

Understanding Why the Behavior Is Happening

Before you can solve a crate problem, you need to identify the likely reason behind it. The same behavior can have different causes. For example, whining may mean your puppy needs a bathroom break, or it may mean your dog is testing whether noise gets attention. Barking can come from boredom, frustration, fear, or excess energy. Resistance to entering the crate may happen because the dog associates the crate with being left alone too suddenly or for too long.

Ask yourself a few simple questions before reacting:

  • Has my dog had enough exercise today?
  • Did my dog recently go to the bathroom?
  • Am I asking for too much crate time too soon?
  • Have I made the crate a positive place with treats, toys, and calm experiences?
  • Does my dog only go into the crate when something unpleasant happens, such as being left alone?
  • Is the room too noisy, bright, hot, or stimulating?

These questions help you avoid treating every issue as disobedience. A tired, comfortable dog with realistic crate expectations is much more likely to settle than a dog who is full of energy, stressed, or confused.

Also consider your dog’s age and history. Puppies have limited bladder control and often need more reassurance. Rescue dogs or dogs with a history of confinement stress may need a slower introduction. Tailoring your approach to the individual dog will speed up progress and reduce setbacks.

How to Handle Whining and Barking

Whining and barking are among the most frustrating crate training issues because they can be hard to ignore, especially at night or in an apartment. The key is to respond thoughtfully rather than emotionally.

Start by ruling out genuine needs. If your puppy has been in the crate for a while and may need to eliminate, take them out calmly and quietly. Do not turn the break into playtime. Keep the lights low, avoid excitement, and return your dog to the crate once they have finished. This teaches your dog that nighttime outings are for bathroom needs only.

If you are confident your dog’s needs have been met, avoid rewarding noise with attention. Many owners accidentally train more whining by opening the crate door, talking soothingly, or hovering nearby whenever the dog cries. From the dog’s perspective, the noise worked.

Instead, use these strategies:

  • Wait for a brief pause in the whining or barking before opening the crate.
  • Reward calm behavior with quiet praise or a treat.
  • Keep crate sessions short enough that your dog can succeed.
  • Provide a safe chew or stuffed food toy to encourage settling.
  • Make sure your dog gets physical exercise and mental enrichment before crate time.

If barking starts the moment the dog enters the crate, go back a step in training. Practice short sessions with the door open. Toss treats inside, let your dog go in and out freely, and gradually build comfort before closing the door for even a few seconds. If your dog only barks when you leave the room, practice very short departures and returns so your dog learns that your absence is temporary and safe.

At night, place the crate near your bed at first if needed, especially for a young puppy. Your presence can be reassuring and may reduce distress. As your dog becomes more comfortable, you can gradually move the crate to its long-term location.

Reducing Anxiety and Building Calm Associations

Some crate issues are not just complaints; they are signs of anxiety. A dog experiencing crate-related anxiety may pant, drool, pace, scratch frantically, bite the bars, or become highly distressed the moment they anticipate confinement. In these cases, pushing through the behavior usually makes it worse. The solution is to slow down and rebuild the crate as a place of safety.

Begin with positive association exercises that do not involve long confinement. Feed meals in or near the crate. Scatter treats inside and let your dog discover them. Offer special chew items that are only available in the crate. Sit nearby while your dog relaxes there so the crate does not always predict separation.

Use a gradual progression:

  • Reward your dog for looking at the crate.
  • Reward your dog for stepping inside.
  • Reward your dog for staying inside briefly with the door open.
  • Close the door for one to five seconds, then open it before your dog becomes upset.
  • Slowly increase duration while your dog remains calm.
  • Add small distance, such as taking one step away and returning.
  • Practice short exits from the room and return before anxiety escalates.

The goal is to keep your dog under threshold, meaning calm enough to learn. If your dog begins to panic, the session is too difficult. Go back to an easier step and build more gradually.

Environment matters too. A crate can feel more soothing if it is placed in a quiet area away from heavy foot traffic. Some dogs relax better with a light crate cover, while others prefer more visibility. Soft background noise, such as a fan or white noise machine, can also help block startling sounds.

If your dog shows severe distress, especially when left alone, you may be dealing with separation anxiety rather than a simple crate issue. In that situation, it is wise to consult a qualified trainer or veterinary behavior professional. Severe anxiety often requires a more specialized plan, and early support can prevent the problem from deepening.

Overcoming Resistance and Refusal to Enter the Crate

If your dog plants their feet, runs away, or stiffens when asked to go into the crate, do not drag, push, or force them inside. Force may create a stronger negative association and make future training harder. Instead, make entering the crate feel like the dog’s choice.

Start by changing what the crate predicts. If the crate only means isolation, the dog has a reason to avoid it. Add positive experiences throughout the day when you are home and not going anywhere. Let your dog find treats in the crate, nap there with the door open, or enjoy a favorite chew inside while you sit nearby reading or working.

You can also teach a crate cue in a simple, upbeat way:

  • Toss a treat into the crate and let your dog go in to get it.
  • As your dog begins to enter reliably, say a cue such as “crate” or “bed.”
  • Reward generously once your dog goes in.
  • Release your dog after a second or two at first.
  • Gradually increase how long your dog stays before release.

Keep your tone cheerful and low-pressure. Many dogs resist because owners only use the crate cue when they are leaving the house. Mix in practice sessions where the dog enters the crate, gets rewarded, and then comes right back out. This reduces suspicion and keeps the cue from becoming a warning signal.

If your dog hesitates at the doorway, check for practical issues. The crate may be too small, unstable, slippery, or uncomfortable. Add a non-slip mat if needed. Make sure the bedding is appropriate for your dog’s preferences. Some dogs like soft cushioning, while others prefer a cooler, flatter surface.

Finally, review your timing. Resistance often appears when owners increase crate duration too quickly. If your dog can handle five minutes calmly, jumping to one hour may trigger refusal. Build in small increments and aim for many successful repetitions rather than a few stressful ones.

Progress in crate training is rarely perfectly linear. You may solve one issue and then see a setback after a schedule change, travel, illness, or a new environment. That is normal. Return to basics, lower the difficulty, and rebuild confidence. Calm repetition and consistency are far more effective than pressure or punishment.

When handled thoughtfully, common crate problems become opportunities to better understand your dog. By addressing the underlying cause and creating positive routines, you can turn the crate into a secure and dependable space your dog accepts with confidence.

Key Takeaways

  • Whining, barking, anxiety, and resistance usually have an underlying cause, not just disobedience.
  • Always rule out real needs first, especially bathroom breaks, exercise, and overstimulation.
  • Do not reward noise by opening the crate during whining or barking; wait for a brief moment of calm.
  • Use treats, meals, chews, and short successful sessions to build positive crate associations.
  • If your dog shows anxiety, slow down training and work below the point of panic.
  • Never force a dog into the crate; teach entry through choice, rewards, and repetition.
  • Severe distress may require help from a qualified trainer or veterinary behavior professional.

Chapter 7: Creating Long-Term Success and Transitioning Beyond the Crate

Crate training is not just about teaching a dog to tolerate a confined space. At its best, it creates structure, supports emotional regulation, encourages independence, and gives your dog a safe place to rest. But the crate is a tool, not the final goal. Long-term success means helping your dog carry those calm habits into everyday life, whether the crate remains part of your routine or gradually becomes less necessary.

As your dog matures and becomes more trustworthy, your role shifts from introducing the crate to using it wisely and eventually deciding how it fits into your dog’s adult life. Some dogs continue to love their crates for years. Others can transition to a gated room, a dog bed, or supervised freedom. The right path depends on your dog’s temperament, age, energy level, and history of destructive behavior or anxiety.

This final chapter will help you maintain the progress you have made, avoid common setbacks, and transition your dog from crate dependence to reliable freedom in a thoughtful, safe way.

Maintaining Good Crate Habits for the Long Term

Even after your dog is fully crate trained, it is important to keep the crate associated with comfort and predictability. Many owners make the mistake of stopping crate practice completely once the dog seems “done” with training. Then, when the crate is needed later for travel, vet recovery, grooming, visitors, moving homes, or emergencies, the dog resists because the positive routine has faded.

To prevent this, continue using the crate in small, low-pressure ways. Let your dog nap there, enjoy a chew there, or settle there while you work nearby. The crate should remain familiar, not something that only appears during stressful moments.

  • Leave the crate available as a resting space if your home setup allows it.
  • Occasionally feed meals or treats in the crate.
  • Practice short crate sessions even after your dog earns more freedom.
  • Keep bedding clean and the space comfortable.
  • Never use the crate as punishment, even with a well-trained dog.

Consistency matters. If your dog still benefits from crating during overstimulating times, such as when guests arrive or during busy family routines, continue using it as a management tool. Long-term success is not about proving your dog no longer “needs” a crate. It is about understanding when the crate still supports good behavior and emotional balance.

At the same time, make sure the crate is not being overused. A dog who spends too many hours confined may become frustrated, under-exercised, or emotionally flat. Adult dogs need movement, enrichment, social interaction, and opportunities to make good choices outside the crate. The crate should support your dog’s life, not shrink it.

Knowing When Your Dog Is Ready for More Freedom

Transitioning beyond the crate should happen gradually, not all at once. Your dog may be ready for more freedom if they consistently stay calm when left alone, do not chew inappropriate items, can settle outside the crate, and understand household boundaries. Puppies and adolescent dogs often seem ready before they truly are, so it is best to test freedom in stages.

Look for these signs of readiness:

  • Your dog can rest quietly outside the crate without constant supervision.
  • They no longer have accidents indoors.
  • They do not panic, bark excessively, or scratch doors when separated from you.
  • They ignore household items like shoes, cords, pillows, and furniture.
  • They respond reliably to cues such as “place,” “leave it,” and “come.”

Start by giving your dog freedom in a small, dog-proofed area rather than the entire home. A kitchen, laundry room, exercise pen, or gated room is often the best next step. Keep sessions short at first. Leave for five to ten minutes, then return calmly. If your dog remains relaxed and the area stays intact, slowly increase the duration.

It helps to set your dog up for success before each practice session:

  • Provide exercise appropriate to your dog’s age and energy level.
  • Offer a bathroom break before confinement or alone time.
  • Leave a safe chew, food puzzle, or calming activity.
  • Remove tempting or dangerous items from the area.
  • Use cameras if possible to observe your dog’s behavior when alone.

If your dog makes a mistake, avoid seeing it as failure. It simply means the transition moved too quickly. Go back one step, reduce the amount of freedom, and rebuild more gradually. Dogs learn best through successful repetition, not through being given too much too soon.

Making the Transition Smooth and Safe

The most successful transitions are structured. Rather than suddenly retiring the crate, blend crate time and freedom time together. For example, your dog may sleep in the crate at night but rest in a gated room during short daytime absences. Or they may spend weekdays in the crate when you leave for errands but enjoy supervised freedom in the evening. This gradual approach reduces stress and gives you a chance to evaluate what your dog can truly handle.

As your dog gains freedom, continue teaching calmness outside the crate. This is a critical step. Some dogs behave well in a crate because the environment limits their choices, but they have not yet learned how to settle when they have access to a room or the whole house. Practice relaxation intentionally.

You can build this skill by:

  • Rewarding your dog for lying quietly on a bed or mat.
  • Using place training to create a clear relaxation spot.
  • Providing regular mental enrichment, not just physical exercise.
  • Keeping departures and arrivals low-key.
  • Avoiding emotional, dramatic goodbyes that increase anticipation.

Household management remains important during this phase. Put laundry away, secure trash cans, block access to unsafe rooms, and keep counters clear if your dog is tempted to explore. Freedom should be earned within an environment designed for success.

If your dog regresses during adolescence, after a move, during schedule changes, or after a stressful event, it is perfectly appropriate to bring the crate back into more regular use. This is not going backward. Dogs often need temporary support when routines change. The crate can provide familiar structure during uncertain times.

Also remember that some dogs should continue using a crate in certain situations for life. Dogs recovering from surgery, dogs traveling in cars, dogs staying in hotels, or dogs who become overwhelmed during parties may benefit greatly from ongoing crate comfort. The goal is flexibility, not permanent crate removal at all costs.

Troubleshooting Setbacks and Building a Lifetime Routine

Long-term success comes from paying attention to your dog’s needs as they evolve. A dog who suddenly resists the crate, becomes destructive outside it, or struggles with alone time may be signaling a deeper issue. The cause could be boredom, insufficient exercise, anxiety, medical discomfort, or simply a routine that no longer fits.

When setbacks happen, ask practical questions:

  • Has my dog had enough physical exercise?
  • Have I provided chewing, sniffing, and problem-solving outlets?
  • Am I expecting too much freedom too quickly?
  • Has anything changed in the home or schedule?
  • Could pain, illness, or age-related changes be affecting behavior?

If your dog shows signs of true distress, such as drooling, frantic escape attempts, self-injury, persistent panic, or severe vocalizing, do not assume more crate practice will solve it. These signs may point to separation anxiety or confinement distress, and professional guidance from a qualified trainer or veterinary behavior professional may be needed.

For most dogs, however, long-term crate success is built on a balanced routine. Dogs thrive when they know what to expect. A stable daily rhythm helps prevent problem behaviors and makes both crate time and free time easier to manage.

A strong routine may include:

  • Morning potty break and exercise
  • Meals delivered through training or enrichment activities
  • Scheduled rest periods
  • Short independence practice sessions
  • Calm evening downtime with the family

As your dog ages, continue reassessing what they need. A young adult dog may enjoy more freedom, while a senior dog may appreciate returning to the crate as a cozy, predictable sleeping space. There is no single right outcome. Success is not measured by whether the crate disappears. It is measured by whether your dog feels safe, can relax alone, and behaves reliably in your home.

By now, you have built more than a crate routine. You have taught your dog how to settle, how to handle short periods of separation, how to trust a predictable structure, and how to succeed in your home. Those skills last far beyond the crate itself.

The crate may remain a lifelong comfort, a temporary management tool, or a stepping stone toward greater freedom. Whatever role it plays, your thoughtful training has created something more important: confidence, security, and a foundation for a well-adjusted dog.
