5 Commands Every Dog Should Know by 6 Months

The first six months of a dog’s life are a powerful learning window. Whether you are raising a brand-new puppy or helping a young rescue settle into your home, this stage is the perfect time to teach the skills that create a safe, confident, and well-mannered companion. The good news is that you do not need a complicated routine or harsh methods to get there. With short, consistent, reward-based training sessions, most dogs can learn a handful of life-changing cues that make everyday life smoother for everyone.

These foundational commands are not about showing off. They help keep your dog safe around roads, visitors, children, food, distractions, and unfamiliar environments. They also build communication and trust. Science-backed dog training consistently shows that positive reinforcement, repetition, and clear timing help dogs learn more effectively and with less stress than punishment-based approaches.

So, what should your dog know by 6 months? While every dog develops at a slightly different pace, there are five essential commands that deserve a place at the top of your training list: sit, stay, come, leave it, and down. Together, these cues support impulse control, responsiveness, and calm behavior in real life.

Below, we will break down why each command matters, how to teach it, common mistakes to avoid, and how to practice in ways that actually stick.

Why the First 6 Months Matter for Dog Training

Early training is not about expecting perfection from a baby dog. It is about building habits before unwanted behaviors become deeply rehearsed. Puppies and newly adopted young dogs are constantly learning from their environment, even when we are not actively teaching. Jumping gets attention? That behavior may grow. Pulling leads to exciting smells? That behavior may repeat. Calm focus earns rewards? That behavior gets stronger too.

By 6 months, dogs benefit from learning simple cues that help them navigate daily life. This is also a time when many owners begin to notice increasing independence, bursts of energy, and testing of boundaries. A strong foundation now can prevent frustration later.

Keep training sessions short and upbeat:

  • Aim for 3 to 5 minutes at a time
  • Practice once or twice daily
  • Use soft, high-value treats or praise your dog loves
  • Train in low-distraction settings first, then gradually add challenges
  • End while your dog is still engaged and successful

Now let’s look at the five commands every dog should know by 6 months.

1. Sit: The Foundation for Focus and Calm

If you teach only one cue first, make it sit. Sit is simple, practical, and incredibly versatile. It gives your dog a clear alternative to jumping, darting forward, or demanding attention. You can use it before meals, at the door, when greeting guests, or anytime you want your dog to pause and focus.

To teach sit, hold a treat close to your dog’s nose and slowly move it upward and slightly back. As your dog’s head follows the treat, their bottom will often naturally lower to the ground. The moment it does, mark the behavior with a cheerful “yes” or a click and reward immediately. Then repeat. Once your dog is offering the movement reliably, add the verbal cue “sit” just before you lure.

Helpful tips:

  • Reward the instant your dog’s bottom touches the floor
  • Practice in different rooms before trying outdoors
  • Use sit in real life, not just formal sessions
  • Release your dog after a second or two so they do not guess when training is over

A common mistake is repeating “sit, sit, sit” while your dog ignores you. Instead, say the cue once, help your dog succeed if needed, and reward the correct response. The goal is to make the cue meaningful, not background noise.

2. Stay: Building Impulse Control and Safety

Stay teaches your dog to remain in position until released. This can be useful at curbs, during greetings, when carrying groceries through the door, or while you prepare food. More importantly, stay helps build self-control, which is one of the most valuable life skills for a young dog.

Start small. Ask your dog to sit or lie down. Say “stay,” pause for just one second, then mark and reward before your dog gets up. Gradually increase duration first. Then add a small step away. Then build distance and distractions over time. Think of stay as three separate skills:

  • Duration: how long your dog remains in place
  • Distance: how far away you can move
  • Distraction: what else is happening around them

Train only one of these at a time in the beginning. If you make the exercise longer, farther, and more distracting all at once, most dogs will struggle.

Always use a release word such as “okay” or “free” so your dog learns that stay lasts until you clearly end it. Without a release cue, dogs often guess when they are done, which can create inconsistency.

If your dog breaks the stay, avoid scolding. Simply reset, make it easier, and reward success. Reliable stays are built through many easy wins, not pressure.

3. Come: The Recall That Can Save Your Dog’s Life

Recall, or coming when called, is arguably the most important command your dog can learn. A strong “come” can prevent accidents, stop a chase, and give your dog more freedom over time. Yet it is also one of the most frequently poisoned cues, because many owners accidentally teach their dog that coming ends the fun.

To build a positive recall, start indoors or in a fenced area. Say your dog’s name, then “come” in a happy tone, and move backward a few steps. Many dogs naturally want to follow motion. When your dog reaches you, reward generously with treats, praise, play, or all three. Make coming to you the best part of their day.

To strengthen recall:

  • Reward every successful response in the early stages
  • Use especially exciting rewards for fast recalls
  • Practice games like ping-pong recall between family members
  • Use a long line outdoors for safety while training
  • Call your dog for good things, not only to leave the park or end play

Avoid calling your dog to do something they dislike, such as nail trims or crate time, unless you can still make the outcome positive. Also avoid repeating “come” if your dog is not responding. Instead, reduce distractions and rebuild success in easier settings.

For many rescue adopters, recall training also helps create a stronger emotional bond. Every successful return teaches your dog that checking in with you is rewarding and safe.

4. Leave It: Preventing Trouble Before It Starts

Dogs explore the world with their noses and mouths. That is why “leave it” is such a valuable command. It tells your dog not to engage with something, whether that is dropped food, trash on the sidewalk, a child’s toy, medication, wildlife, or another tempting distraction.

Begin with a treat in your closed hand. Present it to your dog and let them sniff, lick, or paw. Say nothing at first. The moment they back off, even slightly, mark and reward with a different treat from your other hand. This teaches your dog that disengaging pays. Once your dog quickly moves away from the closed hand, add the cue “leave it.”

As your dog improves, progress gradually:

  • Place the treat on the floor with your hand ready to cover it
  • Practice with low-value items before high-value temptations
  • Reward your dog for making the right choice
  • Generalize to walks, household items, and everyday distractions

“Leave it” is not just about obedience. It is a safety skill. It can help prevent resource guarding situations, accidental ingestion, and impulsive grabbing. It also encourages your dog to look to you for guidance instead of making every decision on their own.

One key point: reward your dog for leaving the item, but do not usually let them go back and take it afterward. Otherwise, the cue may start to mean “pause briefly, then grab it.”

5. Down: Encouraging Relaxation and Better Settle Skills

Down is often overlooked, but it is one of the best commands for promoting calm behavior. A dog in a down position is generally more settled than a dog bouncing in a sit, and the cue can be useful in cafes, vet waiting rooms, family gatherings, and quiet time at home.

To teach down, start with your dog in a sit or standing position. Hold a treat at their nose, then slowly move it straight down to the floor and slightly outward. Many dogs will follow the lure and lower their elbows to the ground. The moment they do, mark and reward. If your dog struggles, try practicing on a soft rug or mat, which can feel more comfortable than a slippery floor.

Once your dog understands the movement, add the verbal cue “down.” Then begin reinforcing short periods of calm in the position. This helps turn down into more than a quick trick. It becomes a useful behavior for settling.

Down is especially helpful for excitable puppies and adolescent dogs who need help learning that quiet behavior also earns attention. Pairing down with a mat or bed can be a great next step if you want to teach place training later.

How to Make These Commands Stick in Real Life

Teaching a cue in the living room is just the beginning. Dogs do not automatically generalize skills to new places, people, or distractions. That means your dog may know “sit” perfectly at home and seem to forget it on a busy sidewalk. This is normal, not stubbornness.

To improve reliability:

  • Practice each command in multiple locations
  • Increase distractions gradually
  • Keep rewards strong when difficulty rises
  • Use management tools like leashes, gates, and long lines
  • Train before your dog is overexcited or exhausted

Consistency matters too. Everyone in the household should use the same cue words and reward the same behaviors. Mixed signals can slow learning.

If your dog is under 6 months and still struggling with one or more of these commands, do not panic. Progress is not always linear. Teething, fear periods, overstimulation, and changes in environment can all affect performance. Focus on short sessions, clear communication, and rewarding success. If needed, work with a qualified positive reinforcement trainer for extra support.

Conclusion: Start Small, Stay Consistent, and Celebrate Progress

The five commands every dog should know by 6 months are not about perfection. They are about building a shared language that supports safety, trust, and everyday good behavior. Sit helps your dog focus. Stay builds impulse control. Come can protect your dog in urgent moments. Leave it prevents trouble before it starts. Down encourages calm and relaxation.

Whether you are raising a puppy, guiding a newly adopted rescue, or simply trying to create a more peaceful home, these foundational commands give you a strong place to begin. Keep training fun, reward generously, and remember that every repetition is shaping your dog’s habits. Small daily practice adds up faster than most owners expect.
