5 Commands Every Dog Should Know by 6 Months

The first six months of a dog’s life are full of fast learning, big emotions, and important habits that can last a lifetime. Whether you are raising a brand-new puppy or helping a young rescue dog settle into a new home, this stage is the perfect time to build a foundation of communication. The good news is that training does not need to be harsh, complicated, or time-consuming to work. In fact, the most effective dog training is clear, consistent, and rewarding.

If you are wondering what to prioritize, start with the basics that support safety, self-control, and everyday life. By 6 months old, most dogs can begin to reliably understand a small set of core cues when they are taught with patience and positive reinforcement. These commands are not about creating a “perfect” dog. They are about helping your dog learn how to navigate the human world with confidence.

Below are the five commands every dog should know by 6 months, why they matter, and how to teach them in a science-backed, approachable way.

Why Training by 6 Months Matters

Dogs are always learning, even when we are not actively teaching them. During the first months of life, puppies and young dogs are especially impressionable. This does not mean there is a strict deadline or that older dogs cannot learn. They absolutely can. But early training helps prevent unwanted habits from becoming deeply practiced.

Teaching a few essential commands by 6 months can help with:

  • Safety around roads, doors, and distractions
  • Polite behavior with people and other dogs
  • Better communication between dog and owner
  • Reduced stress during daily routines
  • Increased confidence in new environments

Modern training science strongly supports positive reinforcement, which means rewarding behaviors you want to see again. Rewards can include treats, praise, toys, or access to something your dog enjoys. This approach strengthens learning, protects your relationship, and helps dogs feel safe enough to try.

1. Sit: The Foundation for Focus and Polite Behavior

“Sit” is often the first cue people teach, and for good reason. It is simple, practical, and useful in dozens of real-life situations. A dog who can sit on cue is easier to manage when greeting guests, waiting for meals, clipping on a leash, or pausing before going out the door.

Just as importantly, sit can become an alternative behavior to jumping, barking, or darting forward. Instead of trying to stop every unwanted behavior directly, you can teach your dog what to do instead.

To teach sit, hold a treat near your dog’s nose and slowly move it up and slightly back. As your dog follows the treat, their rear will naturally lower to the ground. The moment it does, mark the behavior with a cheerful “yes” or a click, then give the treat. Once your dog is offering the movement consistently, add the word “sit” right before you lure.

Keep sessions short and upbeat. Practice in low-distraction environments first, then gradually use the cue in everyday settings. Reward generously at the beginning so your dog learns that sitting pays off.

2. Come: The Most Important Safety Command

If you teach only one life-saving command, make it “come.” A reliable recall can help prevent accidents, stop a dog from running toward danger, and give you peace of mind on walks, hikes, and in fenced areas. Recall is not just a trick. It is a safety skill.

The key to building a strong come cue is making it worth your dog’s while every single time. Start indoors or in a quiet area. Say your dog’s name followed by “come” in a happy, inviting voice. Move backward a few steps to encourage pursuit. When your dog reaches you, reward with something they love: high-value treats, praise, a toy, or a quick game.

One common mistake is only calling your dog for things they dislike, such as the end of playtime or a bath. If “come” predicts fun ending, dogs quickly learn to avoid it. Instead, practice many short recalls that end in rewards and then release your dog back to what they were doing.

Never punish a dog for coming, even if they were slow to respond. You want your dog to believe that getting to you is always safe and rewarding.

3. Down: A Calm, Settled Position for Everyday Life

“Down” teaches more than body position. It helps dogs settle, pause, and regulate their energy. This can be especially helpful for excitable puppies, adolescent dogs, and rescue dogs who are still learning how to relax in a home environment.

Down is useful at the vet, during meals, when visitors arrive, or anytime you want your dog to remain calm for a few moments. For some dogs, down can feel more vulnerable than sit, so be patient and avoid forcing the position physically.

To teach it, begin with your dog in a sit or standing position. Hold a treat at their nose, then slowly move it down to the floor and slightly out in front of them. As your dog follows the treat, they may lower their elbows and chest. Mark and reward the instant they reach the floor. If your dog struggles, practice on a soft rug or mat, where lying down may feel more comfortable.

Once your dog understands the motion, add the verbal cue “down.” Over time, begin rewarding longer periods of calm lying down, not just the initial movement. This helps turn down into a powerful relaxation skill, not just a quick behavior.

4. Leave It: Impulse Control That Protects Your Dog

Dogs explore with their mouths and noses, which means they are constantly tempted by dropped food, trash, unsafe objects, and exciting distractions. “Leave it” teaches your dog to disengage from something and look to you instead. This command is one of the best tools for impulse control and safety.

Start with a treat in your closed hand. Present your fist to your dog and let them sniff, lick, or paw if they want. Say nothing at first. The moment they back off even slightly, mark and reward with a different treat from your other hand. Your dog learns that ignoring the first item makes a better reward appear.

When your dog is consistently backing away, add the cue “leave it.” Gradually increase difficulty by placing the item on the floor under your hand, then uncovered, then using more tempting distractions. Always work in small, manageable steps.

Leave it does not mean your dog is being stubborn if they struggle. It means the distraction may be too hard for their current skill level. Set them up to succeed, and reward heavily for good choices.

5. Stay: Building Patience, Control, and Reliability

“Stay” is valuable because it helps your dog hold a position until released. This can make daily life smoother and safer, whether you are opening the front door, carrying groceries inside, or asking your dog to wait before crossing a street.

A strong stay is built in layers: duration, distance, and distraction. If you increase all three at once, most dogs will struggle. Start very small. Ask your dog to sit or lie down, say “stay,” wait one second, then mark and reward. Release with a separate word like “okay” so your dog learns the exercise has a clear endpoint.

Gradually increase the time before the reward. Then add one small step away. Then practice in slightly busier environments. If your dog breaks the stay, simply reset and make the next repetition easier. This is feedback, not failure.

Remember that puppies and adolescent dogs have developing attention spans. Short, successful repetitions are far more effective than long, frustrating ones.

How to Train These Commands Effectively

No matter which command you are teaching, a few training principles make a big difference. Dogs learn best when the process is clear, predictable, and rewarding.

  • Keep sessions short: Aim for 3 to 5 minutes at a time, especially for young dogs.
  • Practice often: Several mini sessions each day work better than one long lesson.
  • Use high-value rewards: Soft, small treats are often ideal for new skills.
  • Train in stages: Start in quiet spaces before adding distractions.
  • Mark the right moment: Use a clicker or a consistent marker word like “yes.”
  • End on success: Finish with an easy win to keep your dog motivated.

It is also important to remember that behavior is affected by sleep, stress, environment, and development. A 6-month-old dog may know a command perfectly in the kitchen and seem to forget it at the park. That is normal. Dogs do not generalize as automatically as humans do, so they need practice in many different places.

Common Mistakes New Dog Owners Should Avoid

Many training setbacks come from very understandable mistakes. If things are not clicking yet, you are not alone. A few small adjustments can often improve progress quickly.

  • Repeating cues over and over: Say the command once, then help your dog succeed.
  • Moving too fast: Increase difficulty gradually instead of expecting instant reliability.
  • Using punishment: Fear and intimidation can damage trust and reduce learning.
  • Practicing only when you need the behavior: Train proactively, not just in stressful moments.
  • Skipping rewards too soon: Reinforcement keeps behavior strong, especially in early stages.

If your dog is struggling with focus, reactivity, fear, or frustration, consider working with a qualified positive reinforcement trainer. Support early on can prevent bigger behavior challenges later.

Conclusion: Start Small, Stay Consistent, and Celebrate Progress

By 6 months, your dog does not need to know dozens of tricks. What matters most is learning a handful of practical commands that improve safety, communication, and confidence. Sit, come, down, leave it, and stay are core life skills that set the stage for everything else you may want to teach later.

Whether you are raising a puppy or bonding with a young rescue, remember that training is not about perfection. It is about building understanding one repetition at a time. Keep sessions positive, break skills into small steps, and celebrate progress wherever you see it. A dog who trusts you, enjoys learning, and knows these five essentials is already on a great path.
